# Comedy Subgenres and Formats

## Major Comedy Categories

### 1. Situation Comedy (Sitcom)

**Definition:**
- Recurring characters in fixed setting
- Humor from situations and relationships
- Usually 30-minute format (TV)

**Structure:**
- Cold open
- Three acts with commercial breaks
- A story (main plot), B story, C story
- Tag/outro

**Character-Driven:**
- Comic personalities
- Flaws create situations
- Ensemble or star-driven
- Running gags

**Examples:**
- Friends, Seinfeld, The Office
- Parks and Recreation, 30 Rock
- Modern Family, The Big Bang Theory

**Key Elements:**
- Relatable situations
- Consistent characters
- Recurring locations
- Comic repetition

### 2. Romantic Comedy (Rom-Com)

**Definition:**
- Romance story + comedy
- HEA required
- Humor from relationship dynamics

**Structure:**
- Meet cute
- Forced proximity
- Falling in like
- Complication/separation
- Grand gesture/reunion
- HEA

**Comic Elements:**
- Awkward first dates
- Misunderstandings
- Ex's and rivals
- Public embarrassment
- Family interference

**Examples:**
- When Harry Met Sally
- The 40-Year-Old Virgin
- Crazy, Stupid, Love
- Knocked Up

### 3. Satire

**Definition:**
- Uses humor to critique
- Exposes human folly/vice
- Social commentary
- Intellectual humor

**Targets:**
- Politics
- Social issues
- Human nature
- Current events
- Institutions

**Approaches:**
- Irony
- Exaggeration
- Parody
- Ridicule

**Examples:**
- The Onion (news satire)
- The Colbert Report (political)
- South Park (everything)
- Jonathan Swift (A Modest Proposal)

### 4. Parody

**Definition:**
- Imitates specific work/style
- Mocks for humor
- Familiarity required
- Affectionate usually

**Types:**
- Film parodies (Scary Movie, Austin Powers)
- TV show parodies
- Genre parodies
- Specific work parodies

**Key Elements:**
- Recognizable source material
- Exaggeration of elements
- Knowing references
- Affectionate mockery

**Examples:**
- Airplane! (disaster movies)
- Young Frankenstein (horror)
- Spaceballs (sci-fi)
- Galaxy Quest (Star Trek)

### 5. Farce

**Definition:**
- Highly exaggerated humor
- Fast-paced chaos
- Slapstick physical comedy
- Absurd situations

**Elements:**
- Mistaken identity
- Physical comedy
- Sexual innuendo
- Chases and collisions
- Chaos and confusion

**Examples:**
- Airplane!, The Naked Gun
- Marx Brothers
- Buster Keaton
- Some Like It Hot

### 6. Dark Comedy (Black Comedy)

**Definition:**
- Humor in dark subjects
- Taboo topics (death, trauma)
- Inappropriate responses
- Challenges sensibilities

**Topics:**
- Death and dying
- Tragedy
- Mental illness
- Crime and violence
- Taboo behaviors

**Examples:**
- Dr. Strangelove
- Fargo
- Heathers
- American Psycho
- Fleabag

**Balance:**
- Not just shock value
- Truth beneath darkness
- Actually funny
- Purpose beyond offense

### 7. Screwball Comedy

**Definition:**
- Fast-paced verbal wit
- Battle of the sexes
- Class conflict
- Romantic comedy subgenre

**Elements:**
- Rapid dialogue
- Over-the-top situations
- Strong female leads
- Class reversals

**Period:**
- 1930s-1940s
- Great Depression era
- Escape from reality

**Examples:**
- Bringing Up Baby
- His Girl Friday
- The Philadelphia Story
- Some Like It Hot

### 8. Sketch Comedy

**Definition:**
- Short comic scenes
- Premise-based
- Can be unrelated
- Variety show format

**Formats:**
- Live performance (SNL)
- TV show (Key & Peele)
- Internet series
- Troupe performance

**Structure:**
- Premise established
- Heighten quickly
- Punchline/payoff
- Quick exit

**Examples:**
- Saturday Night Live
- Key & Peele
- Monty Python's Flying Circus
- I Think You Should Leave

### 9. Stand-Up Comedy

**Definition:**
- Solo performer
- Direct to audience
- Personal perspective
- Immediate feedback

**Styles:**
- Observational (Seinfeld, Ellen)
- Character-based (Costello, Murphy)
- Political (Carlin, Maher)
- Surreal (Izzard, Posehn)
- Prop comedy (Carrot Top)

**Structure:**
- Opening (hello, establish)
- Bits/routines
- Callbacks
- Big closer

**Examples:**
- George Carlin
- Richard Pryor
- Eddie Murphy
- Chris Rock
- Dave Chappelle

### 10. Cringe Comedy

**Definition:**
- Awkward situations
- Social embarrassment
- Secondhand embarrassment
- Squirm humor

**Elements:**
- Social norms violated
- Inappropriate behavior
- Awkward silence
- Failed attempts

**Examples:**
- The Office (UK and US)
- Curb Your Enthusiasm
- Peep Show
- Fleabag
- The Inbetweeners

### 11. Musical Comedy

**Definition:**
- Music + comedy
- Funny songs
- Parody music
- Comic musicals

**Types:**
- Musical films (Blazing Saddles)
- Comedy albums (Weird Al)
- Song parodies
- Musical comedy shows (Flight of the Conchords)

**Elements:**
- Musical skill + humor
- Clever lyrics
- Musical comedy
- Visual accompaniment

**Examples:**
- Weird Al Yankovic
- Flight of the Conchords
- A Mighty Wind
- The Producers

### 12. Animated Comedy

**Definition:**
- Comedy through animation
- Can do anything
- Visual + verbal humor

**Advantages:**
- No physical limitations
- Exaggerated expressions
- Visual gags
- Fantasy elements

**Examples:**
- The Simpsons
- Family Guy
- South Park
- Rick and Morty
- Shrek

**Examples:**

### Action Comedy

**Definition:**
- Action/adventure + comedy
- Both genres present
- Entertainment first

**Examples:**

- The Pink Panther

### Horror Comedy

- Horror + comedy
- Both scare and amuse

**Formats:**
- YouTube sketches
- Web series
- TikTok/Instagram comedy
- Podcasts

**Characteristics:**

## Format-Specific Comedy

### Feature Films

**Structure:**
- 90-120 minutes
- Three-act structure
- Comic set pieces
- Character arc

**Requirements:**
- Sustainable for feature length
- Character depth
- Emotional arc
- Multiple comic set pieces

### TV Episodic

**Episodic vs. Serialized:**
- Episodic: Self-contained episodes (Simpsons)
- Serialized: Continuing story (Arrested Development)
- Mixed: Both (Seinfeld)

**Season Structure:**
- 6-24 episodes
- Character growth across season
- Running gags pay off
- Season finales raise stakes

### Streaming Revolution

**New Freedom:**
- No time constraints
- No content restrictions
- Binge-watching affects pacing
- Niche targeting

**Examples:**
- Netflix: Arrested Development Season 4, BoJack Horseman
- Amazon: Fleabag, The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel
- HBO: Barry, Insecure

### Considerations

**What Makes You Laugh?**
- Personal taste
- Strengths and weaknesses
- Interests and passions
- Life experience

**Market Considerations:**
- What's popular?
- What can you uniquely contribute?
- Where's the niche?
- What's commercially viable?

**Write What You Know:**
- Your perspective
- Your experience
- Your voice
- Your truth

## Hybrid Subgenres

**Popular Combinations:**

- **Rom-Com + Action:** Knight and Day, The Spy Who Dumped Me
- **Horror + Comedy:** Zombieland, Shaun of the Dead
- **Sci-Fi + Comedy:** Galaxy Quest, Guardians of the Galaxy
- **Fantasy + Comedy:** The Princess Bride, Monty Python and the Holy Grail
- **Mystery + Comedy:** Clue, The Pink Panther
- **Superhero + Comedy:** Deadpool, Guardians of the Galaxy

## Genre Parody

**Specific Targets:**

- **Teen Movies:** Not Another Teen Movie
- **Scary Movies:** Scary Movie franchise
- **Epic Movies:** Epic Movie
- **Disaster Movies:** Airplane!
- **Vampire Movies:** What We Do in the Shadows
- **Spy Movies:** Austin Powers, Kingsman

## Experimental Comedy

**Breaking Form:**

- **Non-linear:** Too Many Cooks
- **Meta:** Community, Fleabag
- **Fourth Wall Breaking:** Deadpool, Fleabag
- **Silent Comedy:** Silent era films, The Artist
- **Improvisational:** Curb Your Enthusiasm, Best in Show

## International Comedy

**Cultural Differences:**

- **British:** Irony, dark humor, wordplay (Monty Python, The Office UK)
- **American:** Energy, optimism, character-driven (Seinfeld, Friends)
- **Canadian:** Self-deprecating, polite (Kids in the Hall, SCTV)
- **Australian:** Dark, irreverent (Chris Lilley, Kath & Kim)
- **Japanese:** Physical, slapstick anime

- Taboo topics

- It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia
- Fleabag
