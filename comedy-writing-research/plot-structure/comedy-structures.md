# Comedy Plot Structures and Pacing

## Comedy Story Structures

### The Three-Act Comedy Structure

**Act I: Setup (First 25%)**

**Opening:**
- Establish status quo
- Introduce protagonist in normal world
- Show what's missing or wrong
- Establish comic tone

**Inciting Incident:**
- Character faces problem/challenge
- Sets story in motion
- Creates comic situation
- Forces character to act

**First Complications:**
- Early attempts to solve problem
- Create more problems
- Establish character flaws
- Comic situations arise

**End of Act I:**
- Character committed to journey
- Stakes established
- Comic premise clear
- Reader hooked

**Act II: Development (Middle 50%)**

**First Half:**
- Character tries to solve problem
- Attempts backfire hilariously
- Complications multiply
- Character flaws get in way

**Midpoint:**
- Major escalation or revelation
- Stakes raised
- Character's worst fears realized
- All seems lost

**Second Half:**
- Desperate attempts
- Escalating chaos
- Rock bottom
- Character must change

**End of Act II:**
- Lowest point
- Character hits bottom
- Must confront truth
- No way out but up

**Act III: Resolution (Final 25%)**

**The Turn:**
- Character has realization
- Growth moment
- New approach
- Faces truth

**The Climax:**
- Confronts problem/conflict
- Uses character growth
- Comic payoff
- Problem solved (hilariously)

**Resolution:**
- New normal established
- Character changed
- Loose ends tied
- Final laugh

## Sitcom Structure (30-Minute Format)

### Traditional TV Sitcom Structure

**Cold Open (1-2 minutes):**
- Teaser scene before opening credits
- Establishes premise for episode
- Usually standalone comic moment
- Hooks audience

**Act One (6-7 minutes):**
- A story (main plot) established
- Introduce conflict/problem
- Set up subplots (B and C stories)
- First complication

**Act Two (6-7 minutes):**
- Escalate main plot
- Develop subplots
- Comic situations escalate
- Characters in deeper trouble

**Act Three (6-7 minutes):**
- All plots converge
- Maximum chaos
- Resolution approaches
- Comic payoff

**Tag/Outro (1-2 minutes):**
- After climax resolution
- Final comic moment
- Call back to earlier joke
- End on laugh

**The "Three Jokes Per Page" Rule:**
- Sitcoms should average 3 jokes per page
- Not literally every page
- Overall density of humor
- Consistent comic engine

### Story Distribution in Sitcoms

**Main Plot (Story A):**
- Primary conflict
- Protagonist's journey
- Gets most screen time

**Subplot (Story B):**
- Secondary character conflict
- Supports main plot thematically
- Gets moderate screen time

**Minor Plot (Story C):**
- Tertiary conflict
- May be one scene or running gag
- Quick comic moments

**The Weave:**
- Stories cut between each other
- Thematically connected
- Converge at climax
- All resolve

## Romantic Comedy Structure

### Rom-Com Plot Framework

**Act I: Setup**

**Meet Cute:**
- Protagonists meet in memorable way
- Immediate dynamic established
- Often antagonistic or awkward
- Attraction hints shown

**Setup Their Lives:**
- Show what's missing in each life
- Establish their flaws
- Their worldviews clash
- Chemistry (or potential) shown

**Inciting Incident:**
- Forced into proximity
- Must work together or interact
- Problem that requires both to solve
- Can't avoid each other

**Act II: The Journey**

**Falling in Like:**
- Get to know each other
- Vulnerability moments
- Chemistry builds
- Admit attraction (maybe to self)

**The Complication:**
- Secret revealed
- Misunderstanding occurs
- External obstacle
- They're pulled apart

**Act III: Resolution**

**The Grand Gesture:**
- One character goes after the other
- Public declaration or action
- Proves love/confronts fear
- Comic moment in gesture

**Reconciliation:**
- Forgive each other
- Acknowledge love
- Comic obstacle to overcome
- Finally together

**HEA:**
- Commitment made
- Future hopeful
- Comic resolution
- Reader satisfied

## Farce Structure

### High-Energy Chaos

**Structure:**
- Fast-paced
- Multiple storylines
- Characters in and out
- Doors slamming
- Mistaken identities

**Pacing:**
- Rapid-fire
- Snowball effect
- Escalating chaos
- Controlled confusion

**Resolution:**
- All threads converge
- Maximum chaos before resolution
- Suddenly everything clears up
- Comic exhaustion

## Sketch Comedy Structure

### Individual Sketch Structure

**The Premise:**
- Comic concept established quickly
- Clear funny idea
- Often one joke explored
- 1-3 minutes typically

**Development:**
- Heighten premise
- Explore implications
- Escalate absurdity
- Push to limit

**Punchline/Ending:**
- Satisfying conclusion
- Call back or twist
- End on laugh
- Quick exit

**Types of Sketches:**
- Premise sketch (one idea)
- Character sketch (funny person)
- Parody (mocking specific work)
- Commercial parody
- Absurdist sketch

## Comic Pacing

### Speed and Rhythm

**Fast-Paced Comedy:**
- Farce, screwball
- Quick cuts
- Rapid dialogue
- Overlapping
- Airplane!, Naked Gun

**Moderate Pacing:**
- Sitcoms, rom-coms
- Room for reaction
- Breathing space
- Character-driven
- Friends, The Office

**Slow-Paced Comedy:**
- Deadpan, cringe comedy
- Awkward silences
- Uncomfortable pauses
- Curb Your Enthusiasm, The Office (UK)

### Pacing Techniques

**Acceleration:**
- Start slow/moderate
- Build speed
- Climax at max energy
- Quick resolution

**Deceleration:**
- Fast then slow
- Contrast creates comic effect
- Pause before punchline
- Let laugh land

**Variation:**
- Vary scene speed
- Some fast, some slow
- Rhythm prevents monotony
- Musicality of comedy

**Pauses:**
- Before punchline
- After punchline
- For reaction
- For tension
- For awkwardness

## Joke Density

### How Many Jokes?

**Sitcom Standard:**
- 3 jokes per page (average)
- Not literally every page
- Overall density
- Consistent humor

**Feature Film:**
- Laugh every 1-2 minutes
- Comic set pieces
- Character humor
- Situational humor

**Sketch Comedy:**
- Joke every 15-30 seconds
- Maximum density
- No wasted moments
- Quick hits

### Jake Placement

**Setup Areas:**
- Establish comic premise
- Create expectations
- Build tension
- Lead to punchline

**Punchline Zones:**
- End of scenes
- Act breaks
- Chapter endings
- Climaxes

**Between Punchlines:**
- Character development
- Plot advancement
- Setup for next joke
- Breathing room

## Timing the Laugh

### Letting It Land

**The Pause:**
- After punchline
- Let audience laugh
- Don't step on laugh
- Wait for silence

**The Call Back:**
- Reference earlier joke
- Later in story
- Creates continuity
- Bigger laugh

**The Rule of Three:**
- First time: setup
- Second time: reinforcement
- Third time: twist/punchline

## Comic Climaxes

### Building to the Big Moment

**Escalation:**
- Raise stakes
- Increase chaos
- Multiply problems
- Heighten absurdity

**Convergence:**
- All storylines come together
- All characters involved
- Maximum comic potential
- Everything at stake

**The Peak:**
- Maximum absurdity
- Can't get any crazier
- Peak tension/chaos
- Ready for resolution

**Release:**
- Problem solved hilariously
- Truth confronted
- Growth demonstrated
- Order restored (new normal)

## Common Structure Problems

### Comedy Structure Issues

**1. Setup Without Payoff:**
- Joke established
- Never referenced again
- Unresolved tension
- Feels unfinished

**Fix:** Pay off your setups
- Call back to jokes
- Resolve comic premises
- Close loops

**2. Too Dense:**
- Joke after joke with no break
- Exhausting
- No time to react
- Loses impact

**Fix:** Vary pacing
- Room for reaction
- Character moments
- Build and release

**3. Too Sparse:**
- Not enough jokes
- Long boring stretches
- Forgetting it's comedy
- Loses audience

**Fix:** Maintain density
- Regular laughs
- Comic tension
- Consistent humor

**4. Funny Beginning, Boring End:**
- Great setup
- Loses steam
- Ending falls flat
- Disappointing

**Fix:** Strong finish
- Save best jokes
- Satisfying resolution
- End on laugh

- Emotional beats + laughs
- HEA required

**Satire:**

- Expose flaw/absurdity
- Comic critique
- Intellectual humor

**Parody:**
- Familiarity with source required
- Mimicry and exaggeration
- Specific references
- Fan service

**Dark Comedy:**
- Taboo subjects
- Inappropriate response

**Farce:**
- Physical comedy
- Mistaken identity
- Fast-paced chaos
- Exaggerated situations

---

**Remember:** Comedy structure serves the humor. The best comedic plots arise from character flaws and the logical but absurd consequences of those flaws. Structure should maximize comic potential while delivering satisfying emotional payoff.
