# Murder Mystery Subgenres

## Overview

Murder mystery is one of fiction's most enduring and popular genres, with distinct subgenres that offer different reading experiences. Understanding your mystery type helps you meet reader expectations while finding room to innovate.

**Why Subgenres Matter:**
- Establish reader expectations (what kind of mystery is this?)
- Provide conventions to follow or subvert
- Help categorize and market your work
- Guide structure, tone, and detective type
- Determine level of violence/gore appropriate
- Shape pacing and investigation style

---

## Major Murder Mystery Subgenres

### 1. Cozy Mystery

**Definition:**
Murder set in a small, close-knit community with minimal violence/gore, focusing on puzzle-solving and character relationships.

**Core Elements:**
- Amateur detective (often with special knowledge/connections)
- Small community setting (village, neighborhood, workplace)
- Minimal graphic violence or explicit content
- Crime occurs "off-stage" or with little detail
- Focus on puzzle and social dynamics
- Comfortable, often charming setting despite murder

**Common Settings:**
- Small towns or villages
- Country manors/houses
- Specialized communities (bookstore, bakery, college)
- Hobby groups (knitting circle, garden club)
- Vacation destinations (resorts, cruises)

**Themes:**
- Community secrets and relationships
- Social order disrupted then restored
- Justice within community
- Appearance vs. reality in polite society

**Character Types:**

**Amateur Detectives:**
- Retired professionals (teachers, librarians, doctors)
- Small business owners (baker, bookseller, florist)
- Hobbyists with specialized knowledge
- Someone with connections to authorities
- Curious, observant, nosy (in charming way)

**Common Suspect Archetypes:**
- The wealthy, unlikable victim (everyone has motive)
- The estranged family member
- The secret romantic rival
- The seemingly perfect neighbor
- The charming newcomer with hidden past
- The overworked, underappreciated employee

**Structure:**
- Murder discovered (often by amateur detective)
- Introduction of community and suspects
- Investigation through social connections and conversations
- Red herrings based on community secrets
- Second murder (often raises stakes)
- Gathering of suspects (dinner party, tea, community meeting)
- Detective reveals killer (explain puzzle solution)

**Conventions:**
- No graphic violence or explicit content
- No children or animals harmed (often help investigate)
- Detective has personal connection to community
- Killer is someone known to community (not random stranger)
- Justice served within community (often arrested quietly)
- Subtle humor and charm
- Romance subplot often included
- Recipes, tips, or community details included

**Tropes:**
- "Murder at the [event]" (bake sale, book club, garden party)
- Bumbling local law enforcement
- Amateur detective smarter than professionals
- Everyone in town had a motive
- Killer underestimates amateur detective
- Cozy setting contrasts with murder
- Second murder when killer feels threatened

**Examples:**
- Agatha Christie's Miss Marple series
- Jessica Fletcher in *Murder, She Wrote*
- M.C. Beaton's Agatha Raisin series
- Louise Penny's Chief Inspector Gamache (borderline)
- Lillian Jackson Braun's Cat Who... series
- Cozy mystery series (bakeries, bookshops, craft mysteries)

**When to Choose Cozy:**
- You want charming, comfortable settings
- You prefer puzzle over action
- You want to explore community dynamics
- You want series potential (recurring amateur detective)
- You want to avoid graphic content

### 2. Hardboiled / Noir

**Definition:**
Gritty, cynical mystery featuring professional detectives in corrupt urban settings, emphasizing moral ambiguity and danger.

**Core Elements:**
- Professional detective (private investigator, disgraced police)
- Gritty urban setting (city's underbelly)
- Moral ambiguity (no clear good/evil)
- Danger, violence, physical threat to detective
- Femme fatales and duplicitous clients
- Cynical, world-weary tone

**Common Settings:**
- Decayingly beautiful cities (Los Angeles, New York, Chicago)
- Seedy offices and bars
- Luxury homes (corrupt wealth)
- Industrial districts, docks, warehouses
- Nighttime, rain, shadows

**Themes:**
- Corruption (political, police, corporate)
- Moral compromise (everyone has secrets/price)
- Appearance vs. reality (nothing is what it seems)
- Justice vs. law (law is corrupt, justice personal)
- Loneliness and isolation

**Character Types:**

**Hardboiled Detectives:**
- Private investigators (down on their luck)
- Disgraced or fired police officers
- alcoholic or damaged past
- Cynical but ultimately moral
- Physically capable (fight when necessary)
- Loner, few real connections
- Code of honor despite cynicism

**Common Character Archetypes:**
- Femme fatale (beautiful, dangerous, duplicitous)
- Corrupt authority (police, politicians)
- Wealthy, corrupt client
- Innocent victim (rare, mostly corrupt victims)
- Loyal but ineffective ally (police contact)
- Hired muscle (threatens detective)

**Structure:**
- Client hires detective (often with lies/omissions)
- Investigation leads to deeper corruption
- Detective beaten or threatened
- Femme fatale manipulates detective
- Revelations of larger conspiracy
- Betrayal by client or ally
- Violent confrontation
- Bittersweet resolution (justice but at cost)

**Conventions:**
- First-person narration (cynical voice)
- Detective physically threatened (beaten, shot at)
- Everyone lies or has secrets
- Sexuality dangerous (femme fatale)
- Money motivates murder (greed, blackmail, leverage)
- Justice imperfect (some corruption remains)
- Ambiguous or unhappy endings common
- Quick, sharp dialogue

**Tropes:**
- "They found the body at..." (opening scene)
- Detective beaten for asking questions
- Femme fatale seduces and betrays
- Everyone has a price
- Killer is someone powerful
- Detective walks alone at the end
- City itself as character (corrupting)

**Examples:**
- Raymond Chandler's Philip Marlowe (*The Big Sleep*, *Farewell, My Lovely*)
- Dashiell Hammett's Sam Spade (*The Maltese Falcon*)
- Film: *Chinatown*, *L.A. Confidential*, *Blade Runner*
- Classic noir films (*Double Indemnity*, *The Maltese Falcon*)
- Modern hardboiled (Robert B. Parker's Spenser, Michael Connelly's Harry Bosch)

**When to Choose Hardboiled:**
- You want gritty, cynical tone
- You want moral ambiguity
- You want danger and physical stakes
- You want to explore corruption
- You want first-person voice
- You're comfortable with violence/sexual content

### 3. Police Procedural

**Definition:**
Mystery following law enforcement professionals using realistic investigative methods and police procedures.

**Core Elements:**
- Law enforcement professionals (detectives, forensic experts)
- Realistic police methods and procedures
- Team dynamics and department politics
- Often multiple cases (main + subplot)
- Focus on how investigation works

**Common Settings:**
- Police stations and squad rooms
- Crime scenes and morgues
- Labs (forensic, DNA, ballistic)
- Various locations (following leads)
- Courtrooms (occasionally)

**Themes:**
- Justice through system (flawed but necessary)
- Professionalism and duty
- Teamwork and trust
- Bureaucracy and politics
- Personal cost of investigating violence

**Character Types:**

**Detectives:**
- Homicide detectives (often partnered)
- Forensic specialists (science-based)
- Profilers (psychology-based)
- Rank matters (rookie vs. veteran)
- Personal issues affect work
- Career ambition and politics

**Team Dynamics:**
- Partner relationships (close or strained)
- Department hierarchy (captain, chief)
- Rivalry with other detectives/units
- Pressure from superiors and media
- Family strain (long hours, trauma)

**Structure:**
- Murder discovered (police called)
- Assignment to detectives
- Initial investigation (crime scene, witnesses)
- Forensic evidence processing
- Following leads (dead ends and breakthroughs)
- Building case (eliminating suspects)
- Procedural obstacles (warrants, bureaucracy)
- Climax (arrest, confrontation, or trial)
- Resolution (case closed, personal reflection)

**Conventions:**
- Realistic police methods (interviews, forensics, surveillance)
- Multiple suspects with investigation of each
- Time pressure (media, supervisors, new murders)
- Bureaucratic obstacles
- Team collaboration
- Often multiple cases (A case + B case)
- Professional language and procedures

**Tropes:**
- "We got a DB" (discovery of body)
- Partner cop with contrasting style (by-the-book vs. maverick)
- Overworked, underfunded department
- Brilliant detective vs. incompetent bureaucracy
- Media pressure and scrutiny
- Political interference
- Second murder confirms first is serial

**Examples:**
- *Law & Order* franchise (TV)
- *CSI* franchise (TV)
- *The Silence of the Lambs* (film)
- *Sherlock* (BBC - modern procedural)
- Michael Connelly's Harry Bosch series
- Ian Rankin's Inspector Rebus series
- Peter Robinson's Inspector Banks series
- *The Wire* (TV - police storylines)
- *Mindhunter* (book/TV - FBI profiling)

**When to Choose Police Procedural:**
- You want realistic investigation methods
- You want team dynamics
- You want to explore law enforcement
- You want multiple cases/subplots
- You want procedural accuracy
- You want series potential (recurring team)

### 4. Inverted Mystery

**Definition:**
Reader knows who committed the murder from the beginning; story focuses on how detective discovers the truth and catches the killer.

**Core Elements:**
- Reader sees murder committed (or knows killer)
- Detective doesn't know
- Cat-and-mouse (killer covers tracks, detective investigates)
- Focus on HOW detective catches killer
- Often psychological (killer's planning vs. detective's deduction)

**Common Settings:**
- Any setting (varies widely)
- Often shows killer's normal life (hiding in plain sight)
- Crime scenes (both during murder and investigation)

**Themes:**
- Appearance vs. reality (killer seems normal/nice)
- Hubris (killer thinks they're smarter)
- Justice inevitably catches up
- Secrets and lies

**Structure:**
- **Opening:** Killer commits murder (reader sees it happen)
- **Investigation:** Detective investigates (killer often helps or misleads)
- **Cat and Mouse:** Killer covers tracks, plants false clues
- **Evidence Builds:** Detective finds contradictions
- **Revelation:** Detective figures it out
- **Climax:** Confrontation and arrest

**Narrative Techniques:**
- Parallel storylines (killer's actions + detective's investigation)
- Killer's POV (shows planning, cover-up, fear of discovery)
- Irony (reader knows secrets detective doesn't)
- Killer watches detective (sometimes becomes perspective character)

**Conventions:**
- Killer introduced early (often sympathetic or charming)
- Detective notices small inconsistencies
- Killer nearly caught (close calls)
- Killer tries to misdirect or frame others
- Detective's persistence unravels perfect crime
- Focus on deductive process (how detective knows)

**Tropes:**
- "Perfect crime" that unravels
- Killer inserts themselves into investigation
- Witness saw something (killer must eliminate)
- Small mistake reveals everything
- Killer underestimates detective
- "I never would have suspected you!"

**Examples:**
- *Columbo* (TV series - masterclass in inverted mystery)
- *Dial M for Murder* (film)
- *A Place in the Sun* (film)
- *Strangers on a Train* (film)
- *Swoon* (film)
- *The Talented Mr. Ripley* (novel/film)
- *Kind Hearts and Coronets* (film)
- *Knives Out* (film - inverted elements)
- *Rear Window* (film - semi-inverted)

**When to Choose Inverted:**
- You want suspense (will they get caught?)
- You want psychological depth (killer's mindset)
- You want to show detective's deductive process
- You want to play with dramatic irony
- You want to explore how lies unravel

### 5. Locked Room Mystery

**Definition:**
Murder committed in apparently impossible circumstances (sealed room, no way in or out), requiring brilliant detective to solve.

**Core Elements:**
- Impossible crime (sealed space, no escape route)
- No obvious way murder could occur
- Brilliant detective required
- Puzzle-focused (how was it done?)
- Clever, often complex solution

**Common Settings:**
- Sealed rooms (locked from inside)
- Isolated locations (island, remote house)
- Impossible situations (no footprints in snow)
- Under constant observation (witnesses see nothing)

**Themes:**
- Reason and logic will solve anything
- Things aren't what they seem
- Human ingenuity (both for crime and solution)
- The "impossible" becomes possible

**Structure:**
- Murder discovered in impossible circumstances
- Explanation of why it's impossible (no exit, no entry)
- Investigation of sealed space
- Elimination of supernatural solutions
- Detective finds small overlooked detail
- Clever solution revealed (often involving trick or misdirection)

**Conventions:**
- Crime scene examined in detail
- Witnesses observed space (saw no one enter/leave)
- Supernatural considered but rejected
- Solution involves physical or psychological trick
- Killer had access or knowledge of space
- Often killer was present from beginning

**Common Locked Room Solutions:**
- Secret passage or hidden door
- Murder weapon rigged (delayed action)
- Victim killed earlier than thought (time manipulation)
- Killer never left (still in room or hiding nearby)
- Killer has access (key, duplicate, locksmith skill)
- Psychological trick (murder appeared impossible but wasn't)
- Mechanical device (killer not present)
- Natural phenomenon explained as supernatural

**Examples:**
- *And Then There Were None* by Agatha Christie (isolation)
- *The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo* (locked room elements)
- *The Hollow Man* by John Dickson Carr (classic locked room)
- *The Mystery of the Yellow Room* by Gaston Leroux
- *The Big Sleep* (locked room elements)
- *Sherlock Holmes* stories (*The Speckled Band*)
- *Doctor Who* episodes (*The God Complex*, *Heaven Sent*)
- *Scooby-Doo* episodes (often locked room or impossible crimes)

**When to Choose Locked Room:**
- You love puzzles and intellectual challenges
- You want to showcase detective's brilliance
- You want clever, surprising solutions
- You're comfortable with complex plotting
- You want classic mystery feel

### 6. Historical Mystery

**Definition:**
Murder mystery set in past time period, with historical details enriching the story and often featuring famous detectives.

**Core Elements:**
- Set in specific historical period
- Historical details and authenticity
- Investigation methods appropriate to period
- May feature famous detectives or historical figures
- Past time period affects plot and detection

**Common Settings:**
- Any historical period (Ancient Rome, Victorian England, 1920s, etc.)
- Historical events as backdrop
- Real historical figures as characters
- Period-accurate locations

**Themes:**
- Universal justice across time
- Human nature doesn't change
- Social issues of the time explored
- Historical attitudes and limitations

**Character Types:**
- Period-appropriate detectives (constables, private inquiry agents)
- Famous detectives (Sherlock Holmes, Hercule Poirot, Brother Cadfael)
- Historical figures as detectives or suspects
- Characters reflecting period attitudes

**Structure:**
- Murder occurs in historical setting
- Investigation using period methods (no modern forensics)
- Historical context enriches plot and clues
- Social/class issues of period affect investigation
- Solution reflects period justice

**Conventions:**
- Historical accuracy (or at least authenticity)
- Period-appropriate investigation methods
- Social class, gender roles affect investigation
- Technology limitations (no DNA, no computers)
- Language and dialogue feel period-appropriate

**Subtypes:**

**Ancient/Classical:**
- *Steven Saylor's Roma Sub Rosa* series (Ancient Rome)
- *Lindsey Davis's Falco* series (Ancient Rome)
- *The Detective of the Supernatural* series (Medieval)

**Medieval:**
- *Brother Cadfael* series by Ellis Peters (12th century)
- *The Name of the Rose* by Umberto Eco (14th century)
- *Catherine LeVendeur* series by Sharan Newman

**Victorian/Edwardian:**
- *Sherlock Holmes* by Arthur Conan Doyle
- *Thomas Pitt* series by Anne Perry
- *William Monk* series by Anne Perry
- *Lady Julia Grey* series by Deanna Raybourn

**Early 20th Century:**
- *Hercule Poirot* and *Miss Marple* by Agatha Christie
- *Lord Peter Wimsey* by Dorothy L. Sayers
- *Phryne Fisher* series by Kerry Greenwood
- *Maisie Dobbs* series by Jacqueline Winspear

**Mid-20th Century:**
- *Jacqueline Winspear's Maisie Dobbs* (post-WWI)
- *Charles Finch's Charles Lenox* series (Victorian)
- *Elizabeth Peters' Amelia Peabody* series (Victorian Egypt)

**Examples:**
- *The Name of the Rose* by Umberto Eco (medieval monastery)
- *The Alienist* by Caleb Carr (1896 New York)
- *Babylon Berlin* series (1929 Weimar Germany)
- *I, Claudius* (ancient Rome with murder mystery elements)
- *The Favourite* (film - historical with court intrigue)
- *Gosford Park* (film - 1930s country house)
- *Sherlock Holmes* (Victorian London)

**When to Choose Historical:**
- You love history and research
- You want period atmosphere
- You want to explore past social issues
- You want to write famous detectives
- You enjoy research and historical accuracy

### 7. Psychological Mystery

**Definition:**
Mystery focusing on WHY the murder occurred, emphasizing killer's motivation and psychological depth over puzzle or police procedure.

**Core Elements:**
- Focus on motivation (why, not who)
- Character-driven (deep psychological exploration)
- Killer's psyche central to story
- Often dark, emotional, traumatic
- Blurs line between mystery and literary fiction

**Common Themes:**
- Trauma and its aftermath
- Madness and sanity
- Family secrets and dysfunction
- Obsession and revenge
- Memory and its unreliability
- The nature of evil

**Character Types:**
- Deeply damaged protagonists
- Killers with tragic/understandable motives
- Complex family dynamics
- Victims with dark secrets
- Detectives with personal connection to case

**Structure:**
- Murder discovered
- Investigation delves into psychology
- Backstory revealed slowly
- Traumatic past explored
- Motivation more important than method
- Emotional resolution as important as justice

**Conventions:**
- Less focus on puzzle/clues
- More focus on psychology/motivation
- Often slower, more introspective pacing
- Dark or disturbing content
- Ambiguous morality (understandable killers)
- Emotional payoff as important as intellectual

**Examples:**
- *In the Woods* by Tana French
- Tana French's Dublin Murder Squad series
- *The Secret History* by Donna Tartt (psychological thriller/mystery)
- *Shutter Island* by Dennis Lehane
- *The Girl on the Train* by Paula Hawkins
- *Gone Girl* by Gillian Flynn
- *Before I Go to Sleep* by S.J. Watson
* *The Silent Patient* by Alex Michaelides
- *Mystic River* by Dennis Lehane

**When to Choose Psychological:**
- You want deep character exploration
- You're interested in psychology/trauma
- You want emotional depth
- You want to blur genre boundaries
- You're comfortable with dark/disturbing content

### 8. Thriller-Mystery Blend

**Definition:**
Fast-paced mystery combining puzzle investigation with thriller elements (danger to protagonist, time pressure, high stakes).

**Core Elements:**
- Faster pacing than traditional mystery
- Protagonist in danger (killer targets them)
- Time pressure (race to stop killer)
- Higher stakes (multiple victims, large-scale threat)
- Action mixed with investigation

**Common Settings:**
- Any setting (varies widely)
- Often multiple locations (chase, pursuit)
- Isolated locations (raising stakes)

**Themes:**
- Danger and urgency
- Justice before more killings
- Personal stakes (detective or loved ones threatened)
- Good vs. evil (less moral ambiguity)

**Structure:**
- Murder (often multiple murders)
- Detective assigned or personally involved
- Investigation with danger
- Killer threatens detective (or someone close)
- Race against time
- Action sequences (chases, confrontations)
- Climax (often violent, saves intended victim)

**Conventions:**
- Pacing faster than traditional mystery
- Detective physically threatened
- Often serial killer or conspiracy
- Time pressure (ticking clock)
- Higher body count
- Action mixed with deduction
- Often saves life at climax (prevents final murder)

**Examples:**
- *The Da Vinci Code* by Dan Brown
- *The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo* by Stieg Larsson
- *The Silence of the Lambs* (thriller-mystery blend)
- Alex Cross series by James Patterson
- Lincoln Rhyme series by Jeffery Deaver
- *Seven* (film)
- *Memories of Murder* (film)
- *Zodiac* (film - procedural-thriller blend)
- *Prisoners* (film)

**When to Choose Thriller Blend:**
- You want faster pacing
- You want higher stakes
- You want action mixed with investigation
- You want to grip readers with urgency
- You're comfortable with violence and danger

## Choosing Your Mystery Type

### Quick Decision Guide

**Choose Cozy if you want:**
- Charming, comfortable settings
- Puzzle-solving over action
- Community dynamics
- Series potential with recurring amateur detective
- Minimal graphic content

**Choose Hardboiled if you want:**
- Gritty, cynical tone
- Moral ambiguity
- First-person voice
- Dangerous protagonist
- Urban corruption

**Choose Police Procedural if you want:**
- Realistic investigation methods
- Team dynamics
- Multiple cases
- Series potential with recurring team
- Procedural accuracy

**Choose Inverted if you want:**
- Suspense (will they get caught?)
- Psychological depth
- Cat-and-mouse game
- To show deductive process
- Dramatic irony

**Choose Locked Room if you want:**
- Puzzle-focused mystery
- Brilliant detective
- Clever, surprising solution
- Classic mystery feel
- Intellectual challenge

**Choose Historical if you want:**
- Period atmosphere
- Historical accuracy
- Famous detectives or settings
- Research-based worldbuilding
- Past social issues

**Choose Psychological if you want:**
- Deep character exploration
- Emotional depth
- Motivation over method
- Dark, literary tone
- To blur genre boundaries

**Choose Thriller Blend if you want:**
- Fast pacing
- High stakes
- Action and danger
- Time pressure
- Wider audience appeal

**Popular Combinations:**

**Police Procedural + Psychological:**
- *Mindhunter* (FBI profiling with psychological depth)

**Historical + Thriller:**
- *The Alienist* (1890s psychology + serial killer hunt)
- *The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo* (historical conspiracy + thriller pacing)

- *The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo* (locked room elements + thriller stakes)

- *The Wire* (gritty tone + procedural detail)

## Subgenre and Marketability

**Reader Expectations:**
- Subgenre sets reader expectations (tone, content, style)
- Cozy readers expect different experience than hardboiled readers
- Mixing subgenres should be done deliberately and signaled clearly

**Categorization:**
- Publishers and booksellers categorize by subgenre
- Amazon categories organized by subgenre
- Series usually stay within subgenre

**Audience:**
- Different subgenres appeal to different audiences
- Cozy: Broad audience, less violence
- Hardboiled: Niche audience, expects grit
- Police Procedural: Large audience, likes realism
- Thriller Blend: Widest audience, crossover appeal

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Wrong Tone for Subgenre

**Problem:** Cozy mystery with graphic violence or hardboiled with cute charm.

**Solution:** Understand subgenre conventions and stick to them (or deliberately subvert).

### Mistake 2: Mixing Without Signaling

**Problem:** Blend subgenres without establishing expectations, confusing readers.

**Solution:** Signal the blend early (title, cover description, opening chapters establish tone).

### Mistake 3: Breaking Core Conventions

**Problem:** Locked room with supernatural explanation. Inverted mystery where reader doesn't know killer.

**Solution:** Understand core element of subgenre and respect it (unless explicitly deconstructing).

### Mistake 4: Amateur Detective in Wrong Setting

**Problem:** Cozy amateur detective investigates serial killer thriller (tonal clash).

**Solution:** Match detective type to subgenre (amateur = cozy, professional = procedural/hardboiled).

## Key Principles

### 1. Respect Reader Expectations

**Subgenre establishes contract with reader.**

Deliver what they expect:
- Cozy readers want charm and puzzle, not graphic violence
- Hardboiled readers want grit and moral ambiguity, not cute charm
- Police procedural readers want realism, not supernatural

**You can subvert expectationsâ€”but deliberately and clearly.**

### 2. Understand Your Subgenre's Core Element

**Each subgenre has one central element:**

- Cozy: Community and charm
- Hardboiled: Cynicism and moral ambiguity
- Police Procedural: Realistic methods
- Inverted: Dramatic irony (reader knows)
- Locked Room: Impossible puzzle
- Historical: Period authenticity
- Psychological: Motivation and depth
- Thriller: Pacing and danger

**Build your mystery around this core element.**

### 3. Use Subgenre to Guide All Choices

**Subgenre affects:**

**Tone and Voice:**
- Cozy: Light, charming
- Hardboiled: Cynical, first-person
- Psychological: Introspective, dark

**Violence Level:**
- Cozy: Minimal, off-stage
- Hardboiled: Gritty, present
- Thriller: Frequent, graphic

**Pacing:**
- Cozy: Leisurely with puzzle focus
- Thriller: Fast, urgent
- Psychological: Slow, deliberate

**Detective Type:**
- Cozy: Amateur, nosy, charming
- Hardboiled: Professional, damaged
- Police Procedural: Team, department

**Resolution:**
- Cozy: Justice restored, community heals
- Hardboiled: Bittersweet, some corruption remains
- Psychological: Emotional resolution as important as justice

### 4. Know When to Blend

**Blend for originality, not confusion.**

**Good reasons to blend:**
- Fresh take on familiar type
- Specific story needs both elements
- You understand both subgenres well

**Bad reasons to blend:**
- Can't commit to one type
- Want "everything" (unfocused)
- Don't understand subgenre conventions

### 5. Signal Subgenre Clearly

**Help readers know what to expect:**

**Title:** Cozy titles sound cozy ("Murder at the Bake Sale"). Hardboiled titles sound gritty ("Blood on the Moon").

**Opening:** Establish tone immediately (first chapter sets reader expectations).

**Description:** Jacket copy, Amazon description clearly identifies subgenre.

**Cover:** Visuals signal subgenre (cozy covers look different than hardboiled covers).
