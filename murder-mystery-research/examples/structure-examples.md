# Mystery Structure Examples

## Classic Three-Act Mystery Structure

### Act I: The Setup (Chapters 1-6)

**Chapter 1: The Hook**
- Introduce detective or create immediate mystery
- Establish setting and tone
- Create questions in reader's mind

**Chapter 2: The Crime**
- Murder committed or discovered
- Key players introduced
- Investigation begins

**Chapter 3: Initial Investigation**
- Detective examines scene
- First clues discovered
- Initial suspects identified
- Questions established

**Chapter 4: The Suspects**
- Each main suspect introduced
- Motives established
- Alibis checked
- Red herrings planted

**Chapter 5: Complication**
- Investigation hits obstacle
- New information arises
- Stakes increase
- Detective commits to solving

**Chapter 6: Act I Climax**
- First significant clue discovered
- Theory begins to form
- Direction set for Act II

### Act II: The Investigation (Chapters 7-15)

**Chapter 7: Deepening Mystery**
- Investigation expands
- New evidence emerges
- More suspects added or eliminated

**Chapters 8-10: Following Leads**
- Each chapter investigates different suspect/theory
- Red herrings pursued
- Clues accumulate
- Subplots advance

**Chapter 11: Midpoint Twist**
- Major revelation or complication
- Everything detective thought is wrong
- New direction required
- Stakes raised significantly

**Chapters 12-14: False Solution**
- Detective thinks they've solved it
- Evidence seems to point one way
- Climax building
- Tension peaks

**Chapter 15: The Reversal**
- Solution proves wrong
- Back to square one (but with more information)
- Time pressure introduced
- Personal stakes peak

### Act III: The Resolution (Chapters 16-20)

**Chapter 16: Regrouping**
- Detective reassesses everything
- Clues reexamined
- New theory emerges
- Final piece needed

**Chapter 17: The Breakthrough**
- Final clue discovered
- Realization occurs
- Everything falls into place
- Killer identified

**Chapter 18: The Confrontation**
- Detective faces killer
- Danger peaks
- Killer tries to escape or strike again
- Truth revealed

**Chapter 19: The Explanation**
- Complete walkthrough of crime
- All clues explained
- Motive revealed
- Method detailed
- Red herrings explained

**Chapter 20: Resolution**
- Killer dealt with
- Subplots wrapped up
- Character growth acknowledged
- Setup for next (if series)
- Satisfying conclusion

## The Inverted Mystery (Howcatchem)

### Structure

**Prologue: The Crime**
- Reader witnesses murder
- Knows who killer is
- Knows how it was done
- May know motive

**Chapter 1-3: Aftermath**
- Body discovered
- Detective arrives
- Investigation begins
- Killer watches/participates

**Chapters 4-12: Cat and Mouse**
- Detective investigates
- Killer covers tracks
- Tension from: Will they get caught?
- Killer almost makes mistakes
- Detective almost catches on

**Chapters 13-15: Mistakes**
- Killer makes error
- Detective notices
- Evidence builds
- Net closes

**Chapters 16-18: Capture**
- Detective figures it out
- Trap set
- Confrontation
- Killer caught

**Chapters 19-20: Resolution**
- Why they did it
- Full explanation
- Justice served

## The Multiple Mystery Structure

### Layer 1: Primary Murder
- Main investigation
- Most chapters focus here
- Primary arc

### Layer 2: Secondary Crime
- Theft, assault, or disappearance
- Related to primary
- Provides breaks
- Connects at climax

### Layer 3: Personal Mystery
- Detective's past
- Personal stake
- Emotional core
- Resolved with main mystery

### Interweaving Pattern
- Chapters 1-3: Primary setup
- Chapters 4-5: Secondary introduced
- Chapters 6-8: Primary + secondary mix
- Chapters 9-10: Personal deepens
- Chapters 11-14: All three converge
- Chapters 15-18: Climax weaves all
- Chapters 19-20: All resolved

## Character-Driven Structure

### Focus: Character Development Over Puzzle

**Act I: Character Introduction**
- Who detective is
- Their world
- Their flaws
- Crime occurs (less focus on method)

**Act II: Character Through Investigation**
- How investigation challenges them
- What they learn about themselves
- Relationships tested
- Personal growth
- Mystery solved through character change

**Act III: Character Resolution**
- Mystery solution reveals truth
- Character changed by truth
- Growth acknowledged
- New understanding

## Scene-by-Scene Breakdown Example

### Opening Sequence (Scenes 1-3)

**Scene 1: Establish Normal**
- Detective's daily life
- Their world established
- Personality shown
- Skills displayed

**Scene 2: Disruption**
- Crime reported/discovered
- Detective called in
- Transition from normal to mystery

**Scene 3: Crime Scene**
- Body discovered
- Initial investigation
- First clues
- Tone established
- Questions raised

### Investigation Sequence (Scenes repeat pattern)

**Scene Type A: Interview**
- Question suspect
- Gather information
- Establish character
- Plant clues
- Create tension

**Scene Type B: Analysis**
- Process evidence
- Make deductions
- Share theories
- Move investigation forward

**Scene Type C: Action/Complication**
- Something happens
- New evidence found
- Another crime?
- Personal threat
- Stakes raised

**Scene Type D: Revelation**
- New understanding
- Theory confirmed or denied
- Direction changes
- Closer to truth

### Climax Sequence

**Scene 1: Realization**
- Final piece clicks
- Truth emerges
- Killer identified
- Plan formed

**Scene 2: Confrontation**
- Face killer
- Danger peaks
- Killer revealed
- Confession or capture

**Scene 3: Explanation**
- Walk through crime
- Explain all clues
- Reveal motive
- Detail method
- Resolve red herrings

**Scene 4: Resolution**
- Justice served
- Characters acknowledge change
- Subplots closed
- Setup future

## Pacing Through Structure

### Fast Pacing Sections
- Opening hook
- Crime discovery
- Midpoint twist
- Climax confrontation
- Final chase/capture

### Moderate Pacing Sections
- Initial investigation
- Following leads
- Building case
- Rising action

### Slow Pacing Sections
- Interview scenes
- Analysis and deduction
- Character development
- Processing information
- Aftermath

### Breathing Room
- After action sequences
- Before major revelations
- Between interviews
- During analysis
- For character reflection

## Subplot Integration Points

### Romantic Subplot
- Introduce Act I
- Complicate Act II (obstacles)
- Resolve Act III (with or without partner)

### Professional Subplot
- Job pressure creates obstacles
- Adds realistic tension
- Shows detective's world
- Resolves with main plot

### Personal Subplot
- Detective's flaw/growth
- Connected to case thematically
- Complicates investigation
- Resolves through case

## Common Structures by Subgenre

### Cozy Mystery
- Strong community setting
- Amateur detective
- Multiple suspects in small pool
- Personal connections
- Lower stakes
- Puzzle focus

### Police Procedural
- Team investigation
- Realistic methods
- Bureaucratic obstacles
- Multiple cases
- Professional stakes
- Process focus

### Hardboiled/Noir
- Lone wolf detective
- Urban setting
- Moral ambiguity
- Femme fatale
- Corruption
- Style focus

### Psychological Thriller
- Mind over clues
- Why over who
- Character depth
- Unreliable reality
- Mental games
- Psychology focus

### Historical Mystery
- Period setting
- Historical methods
- Social context
- Period forensics
- Authentic detail
- History focus
