# Action Structure Examples

Concrete examples of how action stories are structured across different formats and subgenres.

---

## Example 1: Classic Action Film Structure (Die Hard)

### Story Overview
**Premise:** NYPD cop trapped in terrorist takeover of LA skyscraper must stop villains alone.

### Act One: Setup (30 minutes)

**Opening Image:** John McClane arrives at LA airport, tired, nervous about seeing wife.

**Normal World:** Wife's Christmas party at Nakatomi Plaza. John established as:
- New York cop (fish out of water in LA)
- Marriage troubles (separated from wife)
- Regular guy, not superhero
- Barely survives first encounter with terrorists

**Inciting Incident:** Terrorists seize building, take hostages.

**Stakes Established:**
- Personal: Wife is among hostages
- Global: Terrorists will escape with millions

**Break into Act Two:** John escapes initial takeover, alone in building, must act.

**First Action Sequence:** John kills first terrorist, steals gun, establishes he's outmatched but determined.

---

### Act Two: Confrontation (60 minutes)

**Early Act Two:**
- John harasses terrorists (hit-and-run tactics)
- Calls police (but they don't believe him/are incompetent)
- Reveals presence to villain (Hans Gruber)
- Wife's danger raised (terrorists learning who hostages are)

**Midpoint (60 minutes in):**
- FBI arrives
- Hans pretends to be hostage
- John and Hans meet (both don't know who other is)
- Stakes raise: FBI will raid building, putting hostages at risk
- John escapes, but roof rigged to explode

**Bad Guys Close In:**
- FBI helicopter shot down
- John isolated
- Hostages will be executed
- John injured (glass in feet)

**All Is Lost:**
- John has no weapons
- Revealed he's barefoot
- Hans has explosives
- Wife identified as John's wife (personal stakes maximum)
- Seemingly no hope

---

### Act Three: Resolution (30 minutes)

**Break into Act Three:** John finds resolve, prepares final stand.

**Climax:**
- John with two guns taped to back
- Final confrontation with Hans
- Hans falls to death
- Terrorist threat neutralized
- John saves wife

**Resolution:**
- Building damaged but hostages safe
- John and Holly reconcile
- Powell (cop ally) drives them home
- Christmas saved

**Hero's Change:**
- Started as troubled everyman
- Ended as heroic, saved marriage
- Proved himself to wife and self

## Example 2: Superhero Origin (Spider-Man)

### Story Overview
**Premise:** Bitten by radioactive spider, teenager gains powers and must stop villain who killed his uncle.

### Act One: Setup (30 minutes)

**Opening Image:** Peter Parker, nerdy high school outcast, bullied.

**Normal World:**
- Peter's ordinary life (school, crush on Mary Jane, living with aunt/uncle)
- Science field trip where spider bites him
- Discovers powers (strength, agility, wall-crawling)

**Inciting Incident:** Uncle Ben killed by carjacker Peter let escape.

**Stakes Established:**
- Personal: Guilt over uncle's death
- Growing: New villain (Green Goblin) threatens city
- Lesson learned: "With great power comes great responsibility"

**Break into Act Two:** Peter becomes Spider-Man, accepts responsibility.

**First Action Sequence:** Stops thief, fights wrestler, early tests of powers.

---

### Act Two: Confrontation (60 minutes)

**Early Act Two:**
- Peter balances school/Spider-Man
- Green Goblin introduced (Norman Osborn)
- Early fights (Spider-Man vs. Goblin)
- Peter learns Goblin is Norman (friend's father, personal stakes)

**Midpoint:**
- Goblin offers partnership
- Peter refuses
- Battle at the Daily Bugle
- Peter injured, identity threatened

**Bad Guys Close In:**
- Goblin attacks Aunt May
- Mary Jane targeted
- Peter's loved ones at risk
- Peter isolated (can't reveal identity to protect them)

**All Is Lost:**
- Goblin reveals he knows Peter's identity
- Aunt May hospitalized
- Mary Jane taken hostage
- Goblin offers choice: save her or save cable car full of children
- Peter must choose

---

### Act Three: Resolution (30 minutes)

**Break into Act Three:** Peter finds strength, refuses to break

**Climax:**
- Confrontation at bridge
- Saves both (children and Mary Jane)
- Final battle: abandoned warehouse
- Goblin mortally wounded
- Norman dies asking Peter not to tell Harry

**Resolution:**
- Norman's funeral
- Harry vows revenge (sequel setup)
- Peter and Mary Jane's awkward moment
- Peter accepts responsibility

**Hero's Change:**
- Started: selfish, abused powers
- Ended: selfless, accepts duty
- Learned responsibility through tragedy

## Example 3: Martial Arts Tournament (The Karate Kid)

### Story Overview
**Premise:** Bullied teenager learns karate from mentor to face tormentors in tournament.

### Act One: Setup (30 minutes)

**Opening Image:** Daniel and mother move to California, starting over.

**Normal World:**
- Daniel's new school
- Meets Ali (love interest)
- Bullied by Johnny (karate-trained)
- Outmatched, can't fight back

**Inciting Incident:** Johnny and friends beat Daniel up.

**Stakes Established:**
- Personal: Can't live in peace, constant bullying
- Goal: Learn to defend himself

**Break into Act Two:** Mr. Miyagi agrees to teach Daniel.

**First Action Sequence:** Daniel fights Miyagi, loses, shows he needs training.

---

### Act Two: Confrontation (60 minutes)

**Early Act Two:**
- Training begins (wax on, wax off - manual labor)
- Daniel frustrated, doesn't understand
- Miyagi reveals training WAS karate
- Daniel learns technique through discipline

**Midpoint:**
- Tournament announced
- Daniel enters
- Miyagi's backstory revealed (WWII trauma)
- Daniel learns karati is about defense, not fighting

**Bad Guys Close In:**
- Johnny's sensei (Kreese) teaches students to hurt Daniel
- Daniel injured in practice by Kreese
- Doubts whether he can compete
- Miyagi refuses to train him if he'll fight for revenge

**All Is Lost:**
- Daniel quits tournament after argument with Miyagi
- No guidance, no training
- Ali gets back with Johnny
- Daniel alone, discouraged

---

### Act Three: Resolution (30 minutes)

**Break into Act Three:** Daniel returns, Miyagi trains him for tournament, not revenge.

**Climax:**
- Tournament begins
- Daniel advances through brackets
- Final match: Daniel vs. Johnny
- Daniel injured (illegal leg sweep)
- Must use crane technique (finishing move)
- Wins tournament

**Resolution:**
- Johnny respects Daniel
- Kreese attacks Johnny for losing
- Miyagi defeats Kreese
- Daniel and Ali together
- Daniel has confidence and inner peace

**Hero's Change:**
- Started: insecure, bullied, wanted revenge
- Ended: confident, disciplined, fights for defense, not revenge
- Learned balance through karate

## Example 4: Buddy Cop Action (Lethal Weapon)

### Story Overview
**Premise:** suicidal cop paired with family man for unlikely partnership.

### Act One: Setup (30 minutes)

**Opening Image:** Riggs alone in trailer, contemplating suicide.

**Normal World:**
- Riggs: crazy, reckless, suicidal
- Murtaugh: family man, near retirement, "too old for this shit"
- Partners forced together by captain

**Inciting Incident:** Assignment to investigate suspicious death.

**Stakes Established:**
- Personal: Murtaugh's family threatened, Riggs seeks death in action
- Professional: Must solve case

**Break into Act Two:** They pursue investigation together, clash constantly.

**First Action Sequence:** Drug bust shootout, shows their contrasting styles.

---

### Act Two: Confrontation (60 minutes)

**Early Act Two:**
- Investigation continues
- Action sequences: escape from hospital, shootout
- Partnership strained (Murtaugh thinks Riggs is crazy)
- Learn to trust each other

**Midpoint:**
- Connection to heroin operation
- Murtaugh's daughter kidnapped
- Personal stakes maximum
- Riggs reveals his wife's death (why he's suicidal)

**Bad Guys Close In:**
- Villains target Murtaugh's family
- Torture scene (Riggs tortured by villains)
- Both partners pushed to limits

**All Is Lost:**
- Captured by villains
- Murtaugh's family at risk
- Seems hopeless

---

### Act Three: Resolution (30 minutes)

**Break into Act Three:** Partners escape, rally for final confrontation.

**Climax:**
- Storm villain's compound
- Riggs vs. main villain (hand-to-hand)
- Murtaugh saves Riggs
- Villain defeated

**Resolution:**
- Riggs spends Christmas with Murtaugh's family
- No longer suicidal (found reason to live)
- Partnership solidified
- Sequel setup

**Partnership Arc:**
- Started: hating each other
- Ended: brothers, family

## Example 5: Disaster Film (The Day After Tomorrow)

### Story Overview
**Premise:** Paleoclimatologist tries to save son as new ice age destroys civilization.

### Act One: Setup (30 minutes)

**Opening Image:** Ice shelf collapses in Antarctica.

**Normal World:**
- Jack Hall (climatologist) warns of global warming
- No one believes him
- Son Sam in New York for academic competition

**Inciting Incident:** Massive storms begin, climate catastrophe starts.

**Stakes Established:**
- Global: Human civilization at risk
- Personal: Sam trapped in frozen New York

**Break into Act Two:** Jack decides to trek to New York to save son.

**First Action Sequence:** Storms begin, destruction starts.

---

### Act Two: Confrontation (60 minutes)

**Early Act Two:**
- Jack and team travel north
- Sam and friends trapped in library
- Multiple survival situations

**Midpoint:**
- Eye of storm approaches
- Jack warns government (ignored)
- Super-freezing begins
- Sam faces wolves (survival horror)

**Bad Guys Close In:**
- Storm intensifies
- Temperatures plummeting
- Jack's teammates die
- Sam's group facing death

**All Is Lost:**
- Jack alone
- Sam trapped in burning library (for warmth)
- Super-freezing coming
- Seems impossible to survive

---

### Act Three: Resolution (30 minutes)

**Break into Act Three:** Jack presses on alone, finds resolve.

**Climax:**
- Jack reaches library
- Finds Sam alive
- Storm passes
- Rescue helicopters arrive

**Resolution:**
- Survivors rescued
- New ice age begun
- Humanity forced to migrate south
- Jack and son reunited

**Hero's Arc:**
- Started: scientist with theory
- Ended: father who saved son
- Personal triumph amid global tragedy

## Key Takeaways

### Common Patterns

**All Action Stories Follow:**
1. **Setup:** Hero in normal world, threat emerges
2. **Confrontation:** Hero faces escalating challenges, low point
3. **Resolution:** Hero triumphs through action, changed by experience

**All Heroes Have:**
- Clear goal
- Fatal flaw
- Stakes (personal + global)
- Character arc
- Action style consistent with type

**All Villains Have:**
- Clear goal
- Strong motivation
- Threat to hero
- Worthy adversary status
- Defeat through hero's action

### Variations by Subgenre

**Spy Thriller:** More investigation/revelation
**Martial Arts:** Training/tournament structure
**Superhero:** Origin + powers + responsibility
**Disaster:** Nature as enemy, survival focus
**Buddy Cop:** Partnership arc
**Heist:** Planning + execution complications
**War:** Military setting, brotherhood, sacrifice
**Revenge:** Personal stakes, ruthless hero

### Escalation Examples

**Die Hard:**
1. Bar fight (small)
2. Building rooftop (medium)
3. Elevator shaft (larger)
4. Vault explosion (bigger)
5. Final rooftop battle (maximum)

**Spider-Man:**
1. Wrestling match (small)
2. Street robbery (medium)
3. Daily Bugle battle (larger)
4. Bridge rescue (bigger)
5. Warehouse final battle (maximum)

**Pattern:** Each set piece tops previous, building to climax.

---

**Remember:** These are examples, not formulas. Use them to understand structure, then innovate within or break the rules brilliantly.

The best action stories:
- Honor genre conventions
- Deliver spectacular action
- Create characters we care about
- Build to satisfying climax
- Leave audiences transformed
