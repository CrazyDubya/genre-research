# Action Subgenres and Formats

## Major Action Subgenres

### 1. Spy Thriller / Espionage

**Definition:**
Action centered around spies, espionage, international intrigue, and covert operations.

**Key Elements:**
- Spies/agents/operatives as protagonists
- International settings
- Gadgets and technology
- Investigation mixed with action
- Twist and turns
- Double agents and betrayals
- Cat-and-mouse games
- Political/military stakes

**Structure:**
- Investigation and revelation
- Action bursts when discovered
- Intellectual puzzle + physical action
- Personal stakes mixed with global

**Character Types:**
- Elite trained agents
- Masters of disguise
- Skilled in combat and technology
- Often emotionally damaged or detached
- Multiple identities/cover stories

**Examples:**
- James Bond franchise
- Mission: Impossible franchise
- Jason Bourne series
- Jack Ryan films
- Atomic Blonde
- Kingsman series

**Conventions:**
- Gadgets and clever solutions
- Exotic locations
- Casino scenes
- Chase sequences (cars, boats, rooftops)
- Femme fatales or allies with hidden agendas
- Mastermind villains
- Global stakes (world war, terrorism)

---

### 2. Martial Arts

**Definition:**
Action focused on hand-to-hand combat, featuring martial arts disciplines and Eastern philosophy.

**Key Elements:**
- Hand-to-hand combat emphasis
- Martial arts techniques and styles
- Training sequences important
- Honor, discipline, philosophy
- Tournament or personal vendetta
- Skill progression shown
- Often Eastern setting/cultural elements

**Structure:**
- Hero's introduction to martial arts
- Training/development
- Hierarchical opponents (easier to harder)
- Tournament structure or revenge arc
- Final fight vs. master villain

**Character Types:**
- Disciplined fighters
- Students and masters
- Honor-bound warriors
- Often seeking revenge or redemption
- Respect for tradition

**Examples:**
- Enter the Dragon
- The Karate Kid
- Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon
- The Raid
- Ip Man
- Kung Fu Panda (animated)
- Kill Bill (homage/tribute)

**Conventions:**
- Training montages
- Dojo scenes
- Tournament brackets
- Honor duels
- Final fight: hero vs. master
- Philosophy/wisdom amid action
- Respect for opponents

---

### 3. Superhero

**Definition:**
Action featuring characters with extraordinary abilities (powers, technology, or both) saving the world.

**Key Elements:**
- Superpowers or advanced technology
- Secret identities
- Origin story (usually)
- Saving the world/city stakes
- Supervillains with matching powers
- Public spectacle (city-wide destruction)
- Responsibility and sacrifice themes

**Structure:**
- Origin: discovering powers/accepting role
- Learning to use powers
- Villain threatens city/world
- Hero must accept responsibility
- Public battle, massive spectacle
- Hero triumphs, accepts identity

**Character Types:**
- Heroes with powers (flight, strength, speed)
- Tech heroes (armor, weapons, gadgets)
- Aliens/gods
- Mutants/genetic experiments
- Tragic heroes (accidents, experiments)

**Examples:**
- MCU films (Iron Man, Captain America, etc.)
- The Dark Knight trilogy
- Superman films
- Spider-Man films
- Wonder Woman
- The Incredibles
- Hancock

**Conventions:**
- Origin story (first film)
- Discovering powers
- Costume/identity
- Love interest who discovers secret
- Villain with similar/counter powers
- Saving the city/world
- Public battle, massive destruction
- Responsibility themes ("with great power...")

---

### 4. Disaster

**Definition:**
Action centered around surviving natural or man-made catastrophes.

**Key Elements:**
- Nature/disaster as enemy
- Group of survivors
- Multiple perspectives/storylines
- Survival against odds
- Sacrifice and bravery
- Escaping impossible situations
- Large-scale spectacle

**Structure:**
- Disaster strikes early
- Group formation (survivors together)
- Series of survival challenges
- Characters die along the way
- Final escape: few survivors

**Character Types:**
- Experts (scientists, pilots, engineers)
- Ordinary people in extraordinary situation
- Leaders who emerge
- Cowards who redeem
- Victims who don't survive

**Examples:**
- The Towering Inferno
- Poseidon Adventure
- Titanic
- The Day After Tomorrow
- 2012
- San Andreas
- Deepwater Horizon

**Conventions:**
- Warning signs ignored
- Disaster strikes spectacularly
- Group of strangers trapped together
- Expert character warns others
- Selfish character dies
- Heroic sacrifices
- Few survivors escape
- Hope amid devastation

---

### 5. Buddy Cop

**Definition:**
Action featuring two contrasting partners forced to work together.

**Key Elements:**
- Two contrasting heroes
- Reluctant partnership
- Conflicting personalities/methods
- Banter and humor
- Each saves the other
- Partnership solidified
- Action + comedy

**Structure:**
- Partners forced together
- Conflict/bickering
- Learn to work together
- Each has hero moment saving other
- Final confrontation: partnership perfected
- Mutual respect

**Character Types:**
- By-the-book vs. loose cannon
- Old vs. young
- Serious vs. funny
- Elite vs. everyman
- Different races/backgrounds

**Examples:**
- Lethal Weapon
- Rush Hour
- Bad Boys
- 48 Hours
- The Other Guys
- Hot Fuzz
- Men in Black

**Conventions:**
- Reluctant pairing
- Personality clash
- Bickering banter
- One wild, one restrained
- Each saves the other
- Partnership solidified
- Sequel potential

---

### 6. Heist

**Definition:**
Action centered around planning and executing a theft/burglary.

**Key Elements:**
- Team of specialists
- Detailed planning
- Precision execution
- Things go wrong
- Improvisation required
- Clever solutions
- betrayal possible

**Structure:**
- Assembling the team
- Planning the heist
- Execution (goes wrong)
- Complications arise
- Improvisation and adaptation
- Escape with loot

**Character Types:**
- Mastermind (planner)
- Muscle (fighter)
- Hacker (tech)
- Driver (transport)
- Inside man
- Demolitions
- Charm/con artist

**Examples:**
- Heat
- The Italian Job
- Ocean's Eleven
* The Town
- Den of Thieves
- Baby Driver
- The Score

**Conventions:**
- Team assembly montage
- Detailed planning
- Test run/practice
- Execution goes wrong
- Unexpected complication
- Betrayal or double-cross
- Narrow escape
- Retirement or one last job

---

### 7. War / Military Action

**Definition:**
Action set within military conflict, featuring soldiers, battles, and warfare.

**Key Elements:**
- Military setting (WWII, Vietnam, Modern)
- Soldiers as protagonists
- Combat missions
- Brotherhood and sacrifice
- Realistic combat (usually)
- Patriotism and duty
- Anti-war themes possible

**Structure:**
- Soldiers introduced
- Mission assigned
- Deployment into combat
- Battle sequences
- Casualties and losses
- Final mission/confrontation
- Survival or sacrifice

**Character Types:**
- Elite soldiers
- Recruits (innocent, learn war's horror)
- Officers and enlisted
- Veterans and rookies
- Sacrificial leaders

**Examples:**
- Saving Private Ryan
- Black Hawk Down
- American Sniper
- Lone Survivor
- 1917
- Apocalypse Now (war as horror)
- Rambo (action vs. realism)

**Conventions:**
- Band of brothers
- Mission before self
- Sacrifice and honor
- Realistic combat violence
- Casualties (named characters die)
- Anti-war message common
- Heroism amid horror

---

### 8. Revenge / Vigilante

**Definition:**
Action driven by personal revenge, often outside legal systems.

**Key Elements:**
- Personal stakes (someone killed/harmed)
- Justice outside law
- Often brutal, ruthless
- Hero may be criminal or flawed
- Emotional motivation
- Violence as solution

**Structure:**
- Wrong committed (usually murder)
- Hero seeks revenge
- Targets those responsible
- Escalating violence
- Final confrontation
- Revenge achieved (or not)
- Consequences shown

**Character Types:**
- Wronged individual
- Often former law enforcement or military
- Driven by anger/grief
- May be as violent as villains
- Moral ambiguity

**Examples:**
- John Wick (assassin avenges dog/wife)
- Taken (father rescues daughter)
- The Punisher (vigilante)
- Kill Bill (bride revenge)
- Gladiator (revenge for family)
- Mandy (revenge for murder)
- Oldboy (revenge for imprisonment)

**Conventions:**
- Opening murder/loss
- Hero is skilled or becomes skilled
- Ruthless efficiency
- Each target more difficult
- Villain behind it all
- Violent confrontation
- Revenge completed (emptiness?)
- May die achieving revenge

---

### 9. Chase / Pursuit

**Definition:**
Action dominated by chase sequences (cars, foot, vehicles).

**Key Elements:**
- Pursuit as central action
- Escape as goal
- Movement and speed
- Obstacles and complications
- Creative chase solutions
- Often heist or revenge-adjacent

**Structure:**
- Something stolen/someone taken
- Chase begins
- Obstacles (traffic, terrain, police)
- Escalation (faster, more dangerous)
- Environmental factors
- Catch or escape

**Character Types:**
- Driver specialists
- Runners/athletes
- Fugitives
- Law enforcement
- Criminals

**Examples:**
- Mad Max: Fury Road (entire film is chase)
- The French Connection (chase focused)
- Baby Driver (getaway driver)
- The Transporter (driver for hire)
- Drive (wheelman)
- Fast & Furious (heists + chases)

**Conventions:**
- Multiple chase sequences
- Vehicle destruction
- Creative obstacles
- Near misses
- Police involvement
- Crash spectacular
- Final confrontation on foot

---

### 10. Survival

**Definition:**
Action centered around surviving hostile environments/situations.

**Key Elements:**
- Environment as enemy
- Limited resources
- Isolation
- Resourcefulness
- Human vs. nature
- Will to live

**Structure:**
- Stranded/trapped
- Initial survival efforts
- Resources dwindle
- Threats emerge (nature, animals, humans)
- Must escape or be rescued
- Final struggle to survive

**Character Types:**
- Ordinary people in extraordinary situation
- Survival experts
- Those who panic
- Those who adapt
- Leaders and followers

**Examples:**
- The Revenant (surviving bear attack, wilderness)
- Cast Away (plane crash survivor)
* 127 Hours (trapped climber)
- All Is Lost (sailor alone at sea)
- The Grey (wolves, cold)
- Jurassic Park (surviving dinosaurs)

**Conventions:**
- Stranded in hostile environment
- Limited resources (food, water, shelter)
- Nature as threat (weather, animals)
- Resourcefulness and ingenuity
- Some die, some survive
- Final escape or rescue
- Changed by experience

## Action Blends

### Action-Comedy

**Definition:**
Action with humor as major element.

**Key Elements:**
- Spectacular action
- Funny dialogue/situations
- May parody action conventions
- Buddy dynamics common
- Stakes serious but tone light

**Examples:**
- Rush Hour
- The Other Guys
- Hot Fuzz
- 21 Jump Street
- Bad Boys
- Kiss Kiss Bang Bang
- The Nice Guys

---

### Action-Horror

**Definition:**
Action mixed with horror elements/creatures.

**Key Elements:**
- Physical action + horror threat
- Monsters/aliens/undead
- Survival against supernatural
- Weapons and combat
- Scary + exciting

**Examples:**
- Aliens (marines vs. aliens)
- Predator (soldiers vs. hunter)
- Blade Runner (action + noir)
- Army of Darkness (fighting deadites)
- World War Z (zombie combat)
- Resident Evil (zombies + action)

---

### Action-Sci-Fi

**Definition:**
Action set in future/space with advanced technology.

**Key Elements:**
- Futuristic weapons/vehicles
- Space settings
- Advanced technology
- Aliens/robots as enemies
- Military in space
- High-concept stakes

**Examples:**
- The Terminator
- Aliens
- Edge of Tomorrow
- Starship Troopers
- The Matrix (virtual reality action)
- Independence Day
- District 9

---

### Action-Thriller

**Definition:**
Action mixed with suspense/tension.

**Key Elements:**
- Stakes are life/death
- Tension and suspense
- Time pressure
- Twists and turns
- Action + mystery

**Examples:**
- The Fugitive (wrong man action)
- Taken (thriller stakes + action)
- Bourne series (amnesia + action)
- Die Hard (terrorist thriller)
- Speed (ticking clock thriller)

---

## Format Variations

### Feature Films

**Characteristics:**
- 90-120 minute runtime
- Three-act structure
- 4-6 major set pieces
- Character arc
- Budget allows spectacle
- Sequel potential

**Structure:**
- Act One: Setup (25 minutes)
- Act Two: Confrontation (60 minutes)
- Act Three: Resolution (30 minutes)

---

### Television / Streaming

**Characteristics:**
- Episodic or serialized
- 45-60 minute episodes
- Season-long arcs
- Smaller scale per episode
- Character development emphasis
- Multiple seasons possible

**Episodic:** Standalone episodes (procedural)
**Serialized:** Continuing story (arc-based)

**Examples:**
- 24 (real-time action)
- Jack Ryan (spy thriller)
- The Boys (superhero deconstruction)
- Cobra Kai (karate kid continuation)
- Daredevil (Marvel street-level)

---

### Video Game Action

**Characteristics:**
- Player controls action
- Combat mechanics
- Boss battles
- Level progression
- Skill development
- Replayability

**Action Game Types:**
- First-person shooters (Call of Duty)
- Third-person action (Uncharted, Tomb Raider)
- Fighting games (Street Fighter, Tekken)
- Beat 'em ups (Devil May Cry, Bayonetta)
- Stealth action (Metal Gear, Assassin's Creed)

---

### Novels

**Characteristics:**
- Internal narration possible
- Character thoughts
- Detailed description
- Pacing through prose
- No budget constraints
- Series common (thrillers)

**Action Writing in Prose:**
- Short, punchy sentences during action
- Longer descriptive passages between
- Character thoughts/emotions
- Can show consequences in detail
- Internal monologue during combat

**Examples:**
- Tom Clancy novels (military techno-thrillers)
- Lee Child's Jack Reacher series
- Vince Flynn's Mitch Rapp series
- Robert Ludlum's Bourne books
- Matthew Reilly's action-adventure novels

## Choosing Your Subgenre

### Considerations

**What Excites You?**
- Personal passion/interest
- Knowledge base (military, martial arts, spies?)
- Stories you love consuming

**Market Considerations:**
- What's popular?
- What can you uniquely contribute?
- Where's the niche?
- What's commercially viable?

**Write What You Know:**
- Your expertise/experience
- Your perspective
- Your voice
- Your truth

---

## Subgenre Conventions

### Honor or Innovate?

**Honor Conventions:**
- Audience expects certain elements
- Satisfy expectations
- Deliver familiar pleasures
- Execute exceptionally well

**Innovate Within:**
- Subvert expectations
- Fresh twist on conventions
- Unique voice/perspective
- Surprising while familiar

**Break Brilliantly:**
- Know rules before breaking
- Have clear reason for deviation
- Make it better, not just different
- Audience rewards innovation

---

**Remember:** Subgenres are tools, not prisons. Understand conventions, honor expectations, then find ways to innovate within or break the rules brilliantly. The best action feels both familiar and fresh.
