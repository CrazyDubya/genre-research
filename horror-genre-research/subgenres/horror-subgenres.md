# Horror Subgenres

## Overview

Horror is one of the most diverse genres, with numerous subgenres that can blend and overlap. Understanding subgenre conventions helps you meet reader expectations while finding room for innovation.

**Why Subgenres Matter:**
- Establish reader expectations (what kind of horror story is this?)
- Provide conventions to follow or subvert
- Help categorize and market your work
- Guide structure, pacing, and scare design

---

## Major Horror Subgenres

### 1. Supernatural Horror

**Definition:** Horror involving ghosts, demons, spirits, or other supernatural entities.

**Core Element:**
- The threat is supernatural (not natural or human)

**Common Themes:**
- Life after death
- Good vs. evil (religious/supernatural)
- The past returning (ghosts, curses)
- Possession and loss of self
- Divine judgment/damnation

**Subtypes:**

**Ghost Stories:**
- Spirits of dead haunting location/people
- Unfinished business, revenge, or trapped souls
- Examples: The Haunting of Hill House, The Sixth Sense, The Others, The Ring

**Demonic Possession:**
- Demon takes control of human body
- Exorcism as climax
- Religious themes
- Examples: The Exorcist, The Conjuring, Hereditary (ambiguous)

**Curses:**
- Supernatural punishment passed to others
- Inevitable doom (can't escape)
- Rules for spreading/avoiding
- Examples: It Follows, The Ring, Drag Me to Hell, Final Destination

**Haunted Objects/Places:**
- Specific item/location is evil/haunted
- Character must escape or destroy
- Examples: Annabelle, The Shining (hotel), Poltergeist

**Key Conventions:**
- Skeptic character becomes believer
- Research/investigation phase (learning rules)
- Escalating supernatural events
- Confrontation with entity
- Exorcism/destruction/release

**Common Tropes:**
- "Based on true events"
- Psychic/medium as guide
- Innocent victim (doesn't deserve supernatural attack)
- Rules (how entity operates, how to defeat)
- Religious objects (crucifixes, holy water) as weapons/defense

### 2. Psychological Horror

**Definition:** Horror that messes with perception, reality, and sanity. The threat may be real, imagined, or ambiguous.

**Core Element:**
- Unreliable reality (is this real or in character's mind?)

**Common Themes:**
- Madness and insanity
- Paranoia and persecution
- Gaslighting and manipulation
- Trauma manifesting
- The nature of reality

**Subtypes:**

**Madness/Insanity:**
- Character losing grip on reality
- Hallucinations, delusions, paranoia
- Blurred line between real and imagined
- Examples: The Shining, Black Swan, Jacob's Ladder, A Tale of Two Sisters

**Gaslighting:**
- Character manipulated into doubting sanity
- Others deny reality
- Character questions own perception
- Examples: Rosemary's Baby, Gaslight, The Babadook (ambiguous)

**Paranoia:**
- Character feels targeted/persecuted
- May be real or imagined
- Everyone is suspect
- Examples: The Tenant, Bug, Misery

**Trauma Horror:**
- Past trauma resurfacing as horror
- Guilt, grief, PTSD manifesting
- Examples: Hereditary, The Babadook, The Night House

**Key Conventions:**
- Unreliable narrator
- Ambiguous reality (reader unsure what's real)
- Mental deterioration over time
- Isolation amplifies paranoia
- Twist revelation (real or imagined?)

**Common Tropes:**
- "It's all in your head" (may or may not be true)
- Medication/treatment as horror element
- Mirrors/reflections (distorted self)
- Doubles/doppelgangers
- Memory gaps, blackouts

### 3. Slasher

**Definition:** Horror featuring a killer (usually human) stalking and murdering victims one by one.

**Core Element:**
- Relentless, often masked killer hunting victims

**Common Themes:**
- Punishment for transgression (sex, drugs, breaking rules)
- Inevitability (killer always returns)
- Survival of the purest (final girl)
- Revenge or pure evil

**Key Elements:**

**The Killer:**
- Often masked (Michael Myers, Jason Voorhees, Ghostface)
- Superhuman resilience (hard to kill)
- Backstory (usually tragic or evil)
- Motive: revenge, pure evil, or unknown

**The Victims:**
- Usually teenagers/young adults
- Transgressive behavior (sex, drugs, breaking rules)
- Killed one by one (escalating body count)
- First victim establishes threat

**The Final Girl (or Guy):**
- Sole survivor
- Pure/innocent (doesn't transgress)
- Confronts and defeats killer
- Examples: Halloween (Laurie Strode), Friday the 13th (Alice), Scream (Sidney)

**Setting:**
- Isolated location (camp, house, small town)
- No help can arrive
- Trapped with killer

**Key Conventions:**
- Killer introduction (first murder)
- Victims picked off one by one
- False sense of safety (killer not dead)
- Final confrontation (final girl vs. killer)
- Killer's "death" (sequel bait)

**Common Tropes:**
- "I'll be right back" (character dies when alone)
- Sex = death (transgression punished)
- Killer walks slowly but always catches up
- Dead body disappears
- Killer's first-person perspective shots

**Notable Examples:**
- Halloween (1978) - The slasher template
- Friday the 13th - Summer camp massacre
- A Nightmare on Elm Street - Dream killer
- Scream - Meta-commentary on slasher tropes
- Texas Chain Saw Massacre - Gritty proto-slasher

### 4. Creature Feature

**Definition:** Horror featuring animals, monsters, or creatures as the threat.

**Core Element:**
- Non-human creature (natural or mutated)

**Common Themes:**
- Nature's revenge
- Science gone wrong
- Primal fear of predators
- Humans as prey

**Subtypes:**

**Natural Predators:**
- Animals acting aggressively (sharks, wolves, snakes)
- May be normal size or oversized
- Examples: Jaws, Cujo, The Birds, Anaconda

**Mutated/Enhanced:**
- Radiation/science creates monsters
- Unnatural size/intelligence
- Examples: Them! (giant ants), The Host (fish monster), Piranha

**Alien Creatures:**
- Extraterrestrial threats
- Often with unique life cycle
- Examples: Alien, The Thing, Predator

**Cryptids:**
- Legendary creatures (Bigfoot, Loch Ness Monster)
- May be supernatural or just rare
- Examples: The Lost Coast Tapes, Willow Creek

**Mythological/Fantasy:**
- Dragons, werewolves, vampires (as monsters, not romanticized)
- Examples: The Wolfman, Creature from the Black Lagoon

**Key Conventions:**
- Creature introduction (first attack or sighting)
- Research/expert character (studies creature)
- Escalating attacks (creature bolder/more numerous)
- Humans as prey (hunted)
- Final confrontation (kill or escape creature)

**Common Tropes:**
- "Nature gone wrong"
- Creature is misunderstood (rarely true)
- Military/scientists want to capture (not kill)
- Creature's lair (nest, hive)
- Queen/alpha creature (kill it to stop them all)

**Notable Examples:**
- Jaws - The benchmark creature feature
- Alien - Sci-fi creature horror
- The Thing - Shape-shifting creature
- Jurassic Park - Dinosaurs as threat
- Tremors - Underground worm creatures
- The Host - Korean monster movie

### 5. Body Horror

**Definition:** Horror featuring violations, transformations, or aberrations of the human body.

**Core Element:**
- The body itself is source of horror

**Common Themes:**
- Loss of bodily integrity
- Transformation (becoming monster)
- Disease and contamination
- Physical violation (torture, surgery)

**Subtypes:**

**Transformation:**
- Human becoming non-human
- Loss of humanity
- Examples: The Fly, The Thing, American Werewolf in London, Ginger Snaps

**Infection/Disease:**
- Body invaded by virus/parasite
- Physical deterioration
- Examples: 28 Days Later, Contracted, Cabin Fever, The Blob

**Graphic Medical/Torture:**
- Surgery, mutilation, dismemberment
- Often in "torture porn" subgenre
- Examples: Saw, Hostel, Martyrs, The Human Centipede

**Pregnancy/Birth:**
- Reproductive horror
- Unnatural pregnancy/birth
- Examples: Rosemary's Baby, The Brood, Alien (chestburster), It's Alive

**Aging/Decay:**
- Rapid aging or rotting
- Examples: Death Becomes Her, The Neon Demon

**Key Conventions:**
- Body as battleground (self vs. other)
- Graphic description/visualization
- Transformation sequence (usually detailed)
- Loss of identity (body changes = self changes)
- Often no escape (body can't be abandoned)

**Common Tropes:**
- "Get it out of me!" (something inside)
- Mirror scene (character sees transformed body)
- Medical/scientific experimentation
- Contagion (spreads to others)
- Body rebelling against mind

**Notable Examples:**
- The Fly - Scientific transformation
- The Thing - Body invasion and change
- Videodrome - Media-induced body horror
- Tusk - Forced physical transformation
- Raw - Cannibalism awakening
- Teeth - Vagina dentata as body horror

### 6. Folk Horror

**Definition:** Horror set in isolated communities with pagan/ancient beliefs, rituals, and traditions.

**Core Element:**
- Community with sinister traditions/religion

**Common Themes:**
- Clashing beliefs (outsider vs. community)
- Ancient customs/rituals
- Sacrifice (human or animal)
- Nature and seasons
- The old ways vs. modern world

**Key Elements:**

**Isolated Community:**
- Rural village, island, remote town
- Closed, insular
- Outsider protagonist (doesn't understand)
- Examples: The Wicker Man, Midsommar, Children of the Corn

**Pagan/Old Religion:**
- Pre-Christian or folk beliefs
- Rituals, sacrifices, festivals
- Community is deeply committed
- Examples: The Witch, The Wicker Man, Midsommar

**The Outsider:**
- Protagonist from outside
- Doesn't understand community's ways
- Discovers terrible truth
- Often too late to escape
- Examples: The Wicker Man (policeman), The Witch (banished family), Midsommar (grad students)

**Seasonal/Nature Connection:**
- Horror tied to seasons/harvest
- Nature as force/character
- Examples: Midsommar (summer solstice), The Witch (New England wilderness)

**Key Conventions:**
- Outsider arrives in community
- Strange customs/beliefs (unsettling but not yet threatening)
- Gradual realization of true danger
- Outsider trapped (cannot leave)
- Ritual/climax as sacrifice or confrontation

**Common Tropes:**
- "They're not what they seem"
- Community seems friendly at first
- Local festivals/celebrations
- Secrets everyone knows but outsider doesn't
- No one escapes

**Notable Examples:**
- The Wicker Man (1973) - The folk horror template
- The Witch - Puritan New England folk horror
- Midsommar - Bright daylight folk horror
- Children of the Corn - Religious cult community
- Blood on Satan's Claw - 18th century folk horror
- Apostle - Island community with secrets

### 7. Found Footage

**Definition:** Horror presented as "discovered" footage (video recordings, camera phones, etc.).

**Core Element:**
- Pseudo-documentary style (footage is "real")

**Common Themes:**
- Blurred reality (is this real?)
- In-the-moment terror
- Victims documenting their own horror
- Something happening off-camera (scarier)

**Why It Works:**
- Creates immediacy and authenticity
- Feels unscripted/raw
- Limited perspective (what camera shows)
- Off-screen horror (imagined is scarier)

**Key Elements:**

**Reason for Filming:**
- Documentary crew
- Personal journal/diary
- News report
- Security camera
- Examples: The Blair Witch Project (documentary), Cloverfield (party footage), Paranormal Activity (security camera)

**Camera Operator:**
- Character behind camera
- Often dies (footage found)
- Camera as weapon/defense
- Camera dropped (chaos ensues)

**Limited Perspective:**
- Only what camera films
- Off-screen sounds/events
- Poor lighting/shaky cam
- Reader/viewer fills gaps

**Key Conventions:**
- Footage "found" after events
- Often begins normally (then escalates)
- Characters acknowledge camera (at first)
- Camera eventually abandoned (or used for survival)
- Ambiguous or tragic ending (footage runs out)

**Common Tropes:**
- "What was that?!" (off-screen scare)
- Camera drops during climax
- "Why are you still filming?"
- Night vision scenes
- Final shot reveals horror (or cuts to black)

**Notable Examples:**
- The Blair Witch Project - The found footage template
- Paranormal Activity - Domestic found footage
- Cloverfield - Monster movie as found footage
- [REC] - Spanish zombie found footage
- The Last Broadcast - Pre-Blair Witch example
- Cannibal Holocaust - Controversial early example

### 8. Cosmic Horror (Lovecraftian)

**Definition:** Horror dealing with existential dread, cosmic indifference, and humanity's insignificance.

**Core Element:**
- The universe is vast, indifferent, and filled with unknowable horrors

**Common Themes:**
- Human insignificance (we are ants)
- Forbidden knowledge (knowing drives you mad)
- The unknown (things beyond human comprehension)
- Existential nihilism (nothing we do matters)
- Ancient, powerful entities (gods, aliens, forces)

**Key Elements:**

**Unknowable Entities:**
- Beyond human understanding
- Indifferent to humans (not evil, just vast)
- Looking at/understanding causes madness
- Examples: Cthulhu, Azathoth, The Color Out of Space

**Forbidden Knowledge:**
- Learning truth destroys sanity
- Some things should not be known
- Curiosity punished
- Examples: "From Beyond," "The Call of Cthulhu," Event Horizon

**Existential Dread:**
- Humans are insignificant specks
- Universe doesn't care about us
- Our achievements are meaningless
- Examples: Annihilation, The Void, Color Out of Space

**Sanity Mechanic:**
- Characters go mad learning truth
- Madness as horror element
- Examples: In the Mouth of Madness, The Void

**Key Conventions:**
- Investigation (character seeks knowledge)
- Gradual revelation of true horror
- Sanity deteriorating
- Entity/revelation beyond comprehension
- Ambiguous or nihilistic ending

**Common Tropes:**
- "Some doors should not be opened"
- Non-Euclidean geometry (impossible spaces)
- Ancient sleepers (gods from before humanity)
- Cults worshipping entities
- Madness as mercy (ignorance is bliss)

**Notable Examples:**
- The Call of Cthulhu - Lovecraft's template
- The Thing - Cosmic horror in Antarctica
- Event Horizon - Space as cosmic horror
- Annihilation - Alien cosmic horror
- In the Mouth of Madness - Lovecraftian meta-horror
- The Color Out of Space - Latest Lovecraft adaptation

### 9. Religious Horror

**Definition:** Horror dealing with religious themes, demons, exorcism, and divine judgment.

**Core Element:**
- Supernatural evil vs. religious good

**Common Themes:**
- Good vs. evil (religious)
- Faith tested and/or restored
- demonic possession
- Divine judgment/apocalypse
- Sin and punishment

**Key Elements:**

**Demonic Possession:**
- Demon takes over human body
- Exorcism as climax
- Religious struggle for soul
- Examples: The Exorcist, The Possession, The Last Exorcism

**The Antichrist/Apocalypse:**
- Birth of evil child
- End times prophecies
- Examples: The Omen, Rosemary's Baby, Good Omens (horror-comedy)

**Religious Cults:**
- Distorted religious beliefs
- Sacrifice and ritual
- Examples: The Sacrament, Red State, Apostle

**Divine Punishment:**
- God's wrath/judgment
- Sinners punished
- Examples: The Final Conflict, Legions, Constantine

**Key Conventions:**
- Skeptic character (faithless or doubtful)
- Manifestations of evil (physical, supernatural)
- Religious expert (priest, rabbi, exorcist)
- Loss and restoration of faith
- Confrontation with evil (exorcism/battle)

**Common Tropes:**
- "The power of Christ compels you!" (exorcism line)
- Demon speaks in dead languages
- Levitation, unnatural body contortions
- Holy objects (crucifixes, holy water) as weapons
- Faith as ultimate weapon

**Notable Examples:**
- The Exorcist - The religious horror template
- The Omen - Antichrist horror
- Rosemary's Baby - Satanic pregnancy
- The Conjuring - Modern exorcism horror
- Constantine - Supernatural religious action-horror
- The Witch - Puritan religious horror

### 10. Zombie Apocalypse

**Definition:** Horror featuring reanimated corpses (zombies) threatening humanity.

**Core Element:**
- The undead (reanimated corpses)

**Common Themes:**
- Collapse of civilization
- Survival against overwhelming odds
- Humans as bigger threat than zombies
- Infection/contagion
- What makes us human

**Key Elements:**

**The Zombies:**
- Reanimated dead (or infected living)
- Relentless, numerous, unstoppable
- Spreads through bites/scratches
- Examples: Night of the Living Dead, 28 Days Later, Zombieland

**Survivors:**
- Small group of humans
- Diverse personalities (conflict inevitable)
- Resource management (food, weapons, shelter)
- Examples: The Walking Dead, Zombieland, Train to Busan

**Collapse of Society:**
- No government, no laws, no help
- Every person for themselves
- Examples: Dawn of the Dead, World War Z, The Stand

**Safe Zones:**
- Mythical places of refuge
- Often turn out to not be safe
- Examples: Land of the Dead, Warm Bodies

**Key Conventions:**
- Outbreak begins (patient zero)
- Society collapses quickly
- Survivors band together
- Journey to "safe zone" or just survive
- Internal human conflict (humans worse than zombies)

**Common Tropes:**
- "They're coming to get you, Barbara"
- Zombie hoard overwhelming
- Infected member of group (must be killed)
- Humans as real threat (rape, murder, betrayal)
- Finding refuge (temporarily)

**Zombie Types:**
- **Slow shamblers:** Classic Romero zombies (Night of the Living Dead, Dawn of the Dead)
- **Fast runners:** Infected humans (28 Days Later, World War Z, Train to Busan)
- **Supernatural:** Voodoo/religious (The Serpent and the Rainbow)
- **Comedic:** Funny zombies (Shaun of the Dead, Zombieland, Warm Bodies)

**Notable Examples:**
- Night of the Living Dead - The zombie template
- Dawn of the Dead - Consumerism critique with zombies
- 28 Days Later - Fast zombies/infection model
- The Walking Dead - Long-form survival story
- Train to Busan - Korean zombie thriller
- Shaun of the Dead - Horror-comedy blend

## Horror Subgenre Blends

**Horror frequently blends with other genres:**

### Horror-Comedy
- Scary but funny
- Examples: Shaun of the Dead, Zombieland, Tucker and Dale vs. Evil, What We Do in the Shadows

### Horror-Thriller
- Pacing and suspense of thriller with horror elements
- Examples: The Silence of the Lambs, Se7en, The Sixth Sense

### Action-Horror
- Horror threats with action protagonists
- Examples: Aliens, Predator, Army of Darkness, Blade

### Sci-Fi Horror
- Horror in space/futuristic setting
- Examples: Alien, Event Horizon, Pandorum, Life

### Romance-Horror
- Love story with horror elements
- Examples: Twilight (vampires), Warm Bodies, Only Lovers Left Alive, Let the Right One In

### Drama-Horror
- Character-driven horror
- Examples: The Babadook, Hereditary, The Witch

## Choosing Your Subgenre

**Questions to Ask:**

1. **What scares you?** (Your fear will be authentic on page)
2. **What type of threat?** (Supernatural, human, creature, psychological)
3. **What's the theme?** (What does horror reveal about humanity?)
4. **What's the setting?** (Isolated, urban, mundane, fantastic)
5. **Who is the audience?** (Teens, adults, horror fans, general audience)

**Matching Subgenre to Story:**

**If story is about:**
- Past returning → Ghost story / Curse
- Sanity questioned → Psychological horror
- Predatory killer → Slasher
- Science gone wrong → Creature feature / Body horror
- Isolated community → Folk horror
- Existential dread → Cosmic horror
- Religious faith → Religious horror
- Survival against hoard → Zombie apocalypse

## Subgenre Innovation

**How to Innovate Within Subgenre:**

**1. Blend Subgenres:**
- Combine conventions for fresh take
- Examples: Get Out (social commentary + psychological horror), The Babadook (grief drama + supernatural horror)

**2. Subvert Tropes:**
- Use familiar tropes, then twist them
- Examples: Scream (characters know horror rules), The Cabin in the Woods (deconstructs tropes)

**3. New Perspective:**
- Tell story from unusual POV
- Examples: zombie from zombie's perspective (Warm Bodies), monster's POV (may be sympathetic)

**4. Cultural Specificity:**
- Root horror in specific culture/traditions
- Examples: The Wailing (Korean shamanism), Hereditary (grief/trauma specific)

**5. Contemporary Themes:**
- Apply horror to modern anxieties
- Examples: Black Mirror (technology horror), Get Out (racism as horror), The Host (environmental/government horror)

## Key Takeaways

**Subgenres Provide:**
- Reader expectations (what kind of horror story is this?)
- Conventions to follow or subvert
- Structure, pacing, and scare design guidance
- Marketing and categorization

**The Best Horror:**
- Respects subgenre conventions while innovating
- Uses subgenre to explore deeper themes
- Balances familiarity (what reader expects) with surprise (what reader doesn't)
- Creates genuine fear (not just gore or shock)

**Remember:**
Subgenre is a tool, not a cage. Use conventions to build reader trust, then subvert or transcend them for maximum impact.

The best horror stories both satisfy and surprise.
