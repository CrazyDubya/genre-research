# Finding Voice

Voice is what makes writing distinctive. This guide covers understanding, developing, and maintaining voice in fiction.

## What Voice Is

### Defining Voice

Voice is the combination of:
- **Narrative style:** How the story is told
- **Rhythm:** The music of sentences and paragraphs
- **Vocabulary:** Word choice patterns
- **Perspective:** How the narrator/character sees the world
- **Tone:** The emotional color of the prose

Voice is what makes you recognizable on the page.

### Components of Voice

#### Sentence Level
- Average sentence length
- Simple vs. complex structures
- Punctuation patterns
- Rhythm and flow

#### Word Level
- Vocabulary range
- Formality register
- Favorite words/constructions
- Unique expressions

#### Perspective Level
- What the narrator notices
- How observations are framed
- Attitude toward subject matter
- Relationship to reader

#### Tonal Level
- Humor or seriousness
- Warmth or distance
- Irony or earnestness
- Emotional register

## Developing Your Voice

### Discovering Natural Voice

Your voice often emerges when you:
- Write quickly without self-editing
- Write about something you care about
- Write as if to someone specific
- Stop trying to sound like someone else

### Strengthening Voice

Voice becomes stronger through:
- Reading widely (especially outside your genre)
- Writing regularly
- Paying attention to rhythm
- Trusting your instincts
- Editing for voice consistency

### Voice Influences to Study

Study writers whose voice you admire:
- What sentence structures do they use?
- What vocabulary patterns appear?
- How do they handle humor/emotion?
- What makes them distinctive?

Then: develop your own, influenced but not copying.

## Character Voice vs. Narrative Voice

### Narrative Voice

The overall voice of the story:
- Who is telling this story?
- What is their relationship to the events?
- How do they view the world?
- What is their attitude toward the reader?

### Character Voice

Individual character's distinct speech and thought:
- How does this person talk?
- What words do they use?
- What metaphors are natural to them?
- How does their background shape expression?

### POV and Voice

**First person:** Character voice and narrative voice overlap
**Close third:** Narrative voice filtered through character
**Distant third:** Narrative voice more distinct from characters
**Omniscient:** Narrative voice has its own authority

## Voice in Different POVs

### First Person

The "I" voice requires:
- Strong, consistent character voice
- Limited knowledge (only what character knows)
- Character-appropriate vocabulary
- Authentic internal life

**Challenge:** Making the narrator interesting to spend time with

### Third Person Limited

Character-filtered voice requires:
- Maintaining POV character's perspective
- Using vocabulary character would use
- Noticing what character would notice
- Coloring descriptions with character's worldview

**Challenge:** Not slipping into authorial voice or other POV

### Multiple POV

Each POV character needs:
- Distinguishable voice
- Consistent internal voice patterns
- Character-specific observations
- Unique rhythms or vocabulary

**Challenge:** Making POVs distinguishable while maintaining overall story coherence

## Maintaining Voice

### Consistency Within Work

Voice should remain consistent unless shifting for effect:
- Beginning and end should sound like the same book
- Chapter to chapter coherence
- Scene to scene recognizability
- Only shift for deliberate reasons

### Self-Monitoring

Check voice consistency by:
- Reading sections aloud
- Comparing early chapters to later ones
- Having others identify POV from prose
- Noting your own reaction ("this sounds different")

### Voice Drift Prevention

Voice can drift when:
- Writing across long periods
- Your mood changes significantly
- You're influenced by current reading
- You're trying too hard

Prevention:
- Begin sessions by reading previous work
- Maintain awareness of voice characteristics
- Note when something feels "off"
- Have touchstone passages to recalibrate against

## Voice Problems and Solutions

### Problem: Generic Voice

**Symptoms:** Could be anyone; no distinctive quality
**Solution:**
- Inject specific character perspective
- Use more distinctive word choices
- Develop rhythmic patterns
- Trust your natural expression

### Problem: Inconsistent Voice

**Symptoms:** Changes from section to section; no coherence
**Solution:**
- Read beginning before continuing
- Create voice profile to reference
- Revise for consistency
- Note what causes drift

### Problem: Voice Overwhelms Story

**Symptoms:** Style calls attention to itself; obscures narrative
**Solution:**
- Simplify in key dramatic moments
- Let content drive style
- Serve the story, not the voice
- Strategic restraint

### Problem: Trying Too Hard

**Symptoms:** Forced stylization; unnatural constructions
**Solution:**
- Write faster, think less
- Return to natural expression
- Stop emulating specific writers
- Trust what comes naturally

## Voice and Genre

### Genre Expectations

Different genres have voice conventions:
- **Literary:** Voice experimentation welcomed
- **Thriller:** Clear, propulsive voice typical
- **Romance:** Warm, emotionally accessible voice expected
- **Fantasy:** World-appropriate voice important
- **Horror:** Atmosphere-building voice valued

### Balancing Convention and Distinction

Your voice should:
- Serve the genre's emotional goals
- Meet reader expectations
- Still be distinctly yours
- Not fight against genre needs

### Genre Voice Adaptation

Same writer, different genres:
- Core voice elements may remain
- Adaptations serve genre
- Vocabulary may shift
- Pacing may adjust

## Voice in Revision

### Enhancing Voice

During revision:
- Identify your voice strengths
- Amplify what's working
- Smooth inconsistencies
- Strengthen distinctive elements

### Protecting Voice from Over-Editing

Risk: Editing voice out while polishing
- Don't genericize distinctive choices
- Preserve rhythm patterns
- Keep unusual word choices that work
- Trust what makes it yours

### Balancing Clarity and Voice

Sometimes voice and clarity compete:
- Generally: clarity wins
- But: voice shouldn't be sacrificed entirely
- Solution: find clearer ways to achieve voice effects
- Trust: distinctive and clear can coexist

## Practical Exercises

### Voice Discovery
Write the same scene in three different voices. Notice what feels natural.

### Voice Consistency
Read five random paragraphs from your manuscript. Can you tell they're from the same book?

### Voice Distinction
Write dialogue for two characters. Can someone tell them apart without tags?

### Voice Stretching
Try writing in a voice very different from your natural one. What do you learn?
