# Style Enforcement

Style consistency ensures the manuscript maintains coherent voice, tone, and stylistic choices throughout. This guide covers identifying, documenting, and enforcing style across a manuscript.

## What Style Enforcement Covers

### Voice Consistency

The authorial voice should:
- Remain recognizable throughout
- Match genre expectations
- Serve the story appropriately
- Feel intentional and controlled

### Tone Consistency

The emotional tone should:
- Match scene content
- Shift appropriately between scenes
- Maintain overall consistency
- Support genre expectations

### Style Guide Adherence

Technical style should:
- Follow established guides consistently
- Document project-specific choices
- Apply uniformly throughout
- Create professional presentation

## Voice Elements

### Identifying Voice

Voice is composed of:

**Sentence patterns:**
- Average sentence length
- Simple vs. complex sentence preference
- Rhythm and flow characteristics
- Opening patterns

**Vocabulary:**
- Formality level
- Technical language use
- Distinctive word choices
- Metaphor and imagery style

**Narrative distance:**
- Close (intimate, in-character)
- Middle (guided but distinct)
- Far (observational, detached)

**Tonal qualities:**
- Serious/humorous ratio
- Emotional register
- Irony and wit
- Warmth or coolness

### Voice Calibration

Before editing, establish voice baseline:

```
VOICE PROFILE: [Project Title]

SENTENCE PATTERNS:
- Typical length: [Short/Medium/Long]
- Complexity: [Simple/Varied/Complex]
- Distinctive patterns: [Note any]

VOCABULARY:
- Register: [Formal/Casual/Mixed]
- Notable preferences: [Specific words/phrases used]
- Avoidances: [What author doesn't use]

NARRATIVE APPROACH:
- Distance: [Close/Middle/Far]
- POV handling: [Notes]
- Characteristic techniques: [Note any]

TONE:
- Overall: [Description]
- Genre alignment: [How it matches/differs]

SAMPLE PASSAGES:
- Ch. [X], p. [Y]: "[Quote showing characteristic voice]"
- Ch. [X], p. [Y]: "[Another representative sample]"
```

### Voice Drift Detection

Signs of voice drift:
- Sentence patterns suddenly change
- Vocabulary shifts notably
- Tone changes without story reason
- Different "feel" to sections

Common causes:
- Writing across long periods
- Different moods/states during writing
- Attempting to "improve" style mid-project
- Influence from current reading

### Voice Correction

When voice drifts:

1. **Identify the drift**
   - What specifically changed?
   - When did it start?
   - Is it progressive or sudden?

2. **Determine the "true" voice**
   - What establishes the baseline?
   - What serves the story best?
   - What is most sustainable?

3. **Mark inconsistencies**
   - Note passages that deviate
   - Explain the deviation
   - Suggest calibration to baseline

4. **Preserve intentional variation**
   - Scene demands may require shifts
   - Character POV may vary
   - Emotional moments may differ

## Tone Management

### Tone Types

| Tone | Characteristics |
|------|-----------------|
| Light | Upbeat, humorous, low stakes feeling |
| Dark | Serious, heavy, high emotional weight |
| Suspenseful | Tense, uncertain, threat-aware |
| Romantic | Warm, emotionally focused, relationship-centered |
| Ironic | Detached, knowing, subtext-rich |
| Earnest | Sincere, emotionally direct, unironic |

### Appropriate Tone Shifts

Tone should shift when:
- Scene content demands it
- Character emotion changes
- Story phase changes
- Reader experience needs it

Shifts should:
- Be deliberate
- Transition smoothly
- Serve story purpose
- Return appropriately

### Tone Problems

**Tonal whiplash:**
- Abrupt shift without transition
- Reader disorientation
- Undermines emotional investment
**Fix:** Add transitional content; smooth the shift

**Tonal inconsistency:**
- Same type of scene varies in tone
- No clear reason for differences
- Feels uncontrolled
**Fix:** Establish baseline; calibrate deviations

**Tonal mismatch:**
- Tone doesn't serve content
- Comedy in tragedy (unless intentional)
- Lightness in serious moments
**Fix:** Align tone to story needs

## Style Sheet Creation

### Core Components

```
STYLE SHEET: [Project Title]

GENERAL PREFERENCES:
- Serial comma: [Yes/No]
- Singular they: [Yes/No]
- Numbers: [Spell out through ___]
- Time format: [Style]
- Date format: [Style]

PUNCTUATION:
- Dialogue attribution: [Style]
- Em dash spacing: [Style]
- Ellipsis format: [Style]

CAPITALIZATION:
- [List project-specific decisions]

ITALICS USED FOR:
- [List applications]

CHARACTER NAMES:
- [Full name]: [Nickname(s)], [Title if any]
- [Continue for all named characters]

PLACE NAMES:
- [Location]: [Spelling/capitalization]
- [Continue for all named places]

SPECIALIZED TERMS:
- [Term]: [Spelling/capitalization/definition]
- [Continue for all]

STYLE DECISIONS:
- [Issue]: [Decision made]
- [Continue as needed]
```

### Documenting Decisions

For each style decision, record:
- What the issue was
- What was decided
- Why (if helpful)
- Where first applied (chapter reference)

### Applying Consistently

**First occurrence:** Make decision, document
**Subsequent occurrences:** Apply documented decision
**Conflicts:** Resolve in favor of consistency
**Exceptions:** Document and justify

## Genre-Specific Style

### Genre Expectations

Each genre has style conventions:

**Literary fiction:**
- More complex prose acceptable
- Voice experimentation welcomed
- Slower pacing standard
- Subtext valued

**Commercial fiction:**
- Accessible prose preferred
- Clear storytelling prioritized
- Pacing expectations genre-specific
- Direct engagement valued

**Genre fiction (romance, thriller, etc.):**
- Genre conventions expected
- Pacing norms established
- Reader expectations specific
- Tropes and style often linked

### Balancing Voice and Genre

The author's voice must work within genre context:

**Questions to ask:**
- Does this voice serve this genre's readers?
- Does it meet reader expectations while being distinctive?
- Are deviations from convention intentional and effective?
- Does style enhance or hinder genre experience?

## POV Style Consistency

### Single POV

Maintain consistency in:
- Narrative voice throughout
- Access to thoughts/feelings
- Information available
- Language appropriate to character

### Multiple POV

Each POV should:
- Be distinguishable
- Maintain its own consistency
- Not bleed into other POVs
- Have documented voice profile

**Common problems:**
- POVs sound too similar
- POV-inappropriate knowledge
- Narrative voice overriding character voice
- Inconsistent access to interiority

## Style Enforcement Process

### Setup

1. Read full manuscript for voice
2. Create voice profile
3. Establish style sheet
4. Note genre expectations

### During Editing

1. Flag voice deviations
2. Note tone inconsistencies
3. Apply style sheet consistently
4. Update style sheet as needed
5. Track patterns for global notes

### Reporting

Include in feedback:
- Voice assessment
- Tone observations
- Style sheet
- Consistency issues found
- Recommendations

## Style Queries

### Framing Style Feedback

**For voice issues:**
"This passage feels different from the established voice. The sentence structure is more complex, and the vocabulary more formal than elsewhere. Consider whether this shift is intentionalâ€”if so, the transition could be smoother. If not, consider revising toward the lighter, more direct voice established earlier."

**For tone issues:**
"The humor in this scene undercuts the emotional weight of the previous chapter. If the tonal shift is intentional (perhaps the character uses humor to deflect), consider adding a beat that acknowledges this. If the light tone wasn't intentional, the scene might benefit from more emotional weight."

**For style inconsistencies:**
"Note: 'grey' is used here, but 'gray' appears elsewhere. Please confirm which spelling to use throughout; I'll apply consistently."
